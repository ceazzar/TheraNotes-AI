# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Luke presents with a complex psychosocial and neurodevelopmental profile that significantly impacts her capacity to manage daily life in a consistent, safe and sustainable manner. She has diagnoses of Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), Complex Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (CPTSD) and Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD). The interaction of these conditions affects emotional regulation, interpersonal functioning, sensory processing and the ability to establish and maintain daily routines.

Luke experiences persistent and substantial difficulties with emotional regulation, managing distress, navigating social interactions and maintaining functional engagement in daily activities. These challenges are chronic in nature and have contributed to repeated periods of crisis escalation, frequent presentations to emergency departments and multiple psychiatric admissions over the past year. Her presentation reflects the cumulative impact of prolonged stress, emotional exhaustion and reduced functional resilience, rather than isolated or situational episodes of difficulty.

Luke has a history of exceptionally high academic and occupational functioning, having previously worked in the medical field. She retains strong intellectual ability, memory and analytical skills however, her current functional capacity does not reflect this previous level of performance. Importantly, with appropriate, consistent and skilled supports in place, Luke has a strong capacity to regain and sustain higher levels of functional participation across daily living, community engagement and vocational roles. The current discrepancy between her previous functioning and present capacity reflects insufficient support rather than loss of ability. At present, the combined demands of daily living, emotional regulation and self-management exceed her available coping resources, resulting in marked variability in functioning and increased reliance on crisis-based systems of care.

## Social Background {#social-background}

Luke lives alone and experiences complete social isolation. She has no contact with family due to a longstanding history of abuse, estrangement and safety concerns. She does not have friends or community connections who are able to provide emotional, social or practical support. At times, Luke reports going extended periods without leaving her home or engaging in meaningful social interaction.

This absence of social connection has had a significant impact on Luke’s mental health, emotional wellbeing and functional capacity. Without opportunities for co-regulation, external structure or informal monitoring of her wellbeing, Luke is required to manage all aspects of daily living independently, despite fluctuating capacity and high levels of distress. Social isolation has contributed to loss of routine, reduced engagement in meaningful activity and increased vulnerability to emotional escalation.

Luke has identified a strong long-term goal of returning to work. This goal is closely linked to her sense of identity, purpose and financial stability. However, at present, her ability to pursue vocational goals is limited by instability in housing, insufficient daily supports, ongoing mental health crises and the need to rebuild foundational routines and emotional regulation skills before sustainable employment can be considered.

# **Part B: Mental Health And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-health-and-psychosocial-functioning}

Luke’s mental health and psychosocial functioning are significantly influenced by the interaction between her neurodevelopmental profile, complex trauma history, sensory processing differences and prolonged social isolation. These factors collectively affect her ability to regulate emotions, maintain routines, engage with others and sustain participation in daily life. While Luke demonstrates high insight and intellectual capacity, her emotional and functional resources are frequently exceeded by the cumulative demands of everyday living.

## Emotional Regulation {#emotional-regulation}

Luke experiences ongoing and severe difficulty with emotional regulation. Her capacity to manage distress fluctuates significantly throughout the day and week and is highly dependent on environmental conditions, sensory load and the presence of external support. She reports feeling in a state of crisis for much of the day, with limited capacity to recover once overwhelmed.

Periods of emotional escalation are often triggered by relatively routine demands, including interpersonal interactions, decision-making, administrative tasks, sensory exposure or unexpected changes. Once distressed, Luke experiences rapid escalation in emotional intensity and has difficulty accessing self-soothing or grounding strategies independently. During these periods, her ability to problem-solve, communicate effectively and complete even basic tasks is significantly reduced.

Luke reports that evenings and weekends are particularly high-risk times for emotional escalation. During these periods, formal supports are limited or unavailable, daily structure is reduced and fatigue from managing the day accumulates. In the absence of timely support, distress often escalates to crisis point, resulting in emergency department presentations. 

Over the past year, Luke has experienced frequent ED presentations, averaging approximately once per week, alongside multiple inpatient psychiatric admissions. These admissions are typically short in duration and provide limited functional benefit, highlighting the need for proactive, community-based support rather than reactive crisis intervention.

In addition, Luke reports ongoing engagement in self-harm and suicidal behaviours, particularly during periods of emotional escalation. These behaviours are not attention-seeking in nature but occur in the context of severe emotional distress, limited coping capacity and absence of timely support. At present, escalation of these behaviours frequently results in presentation to emergency departments, which Luke experiences as highly distressing and at times, retraumatising.

The current reliance on emergency services reflects a lack of accessible, preventative, community-based supports rather than an absence of insight or willingness to engage in safer strategies. Luke has identified a strong need for alternative approaches to managing periods of acute distress, including proactive support and skill development to reduce reliance on hospital-based crisis responses.

## Psychosocial Functioning {#psychosocial-functioning}

Luke’s psychosocial functioning is significantly impacted by profound social isolation and difficulty navigating interpersonal interactions. She has no family or informal supports and limited social contact outside of professional services. Interactions with others require substantial emotional and cognitive effort and are often experienced as overwhelming rather than supportive.

Luke reports difficulty interpreting social cues, managing conversational flow and understanding others’ intentions, particularly in emotionally charged or unfamiliar situations. She describes a tendency to be perceived as overly direct or rude, despite not intending to cause offence. These experiences have contributed to anxiety around social interaction and avoidance of situations that require communication or negotiation.

When overwhelmed, Luke may experience periods of reduced verbal output or become temporarily non-verbal, limiting her ability to express needs, advocate for herself or seek help. These communication shutdowns occur intermittently and are more likely during periods of high emotional load or sensory overwhelm. As a result, Luke often withdraws further during times when support is most needed, increasing her vulnerability to crisis escalation.

The absence of consistent, supportive interpersonal relationships has also impacted Luke’s sense of identity and belonging. Much of her daily functioning is focused on survival and crisis management, leaving little capacity for engagement in meaningful or restorative activities. Luke reports difficulty identifying hobbies, interests or occupations that provide enjoyment, as her energy is consumed by managing distress and meeting basic needs.

## Cognitive Functioning and Executive Skills {#cognitive-functioning-and-executive-skills}

Luke demonstrates intact and, in some areas, exceptional cognitive ability. She reports strong memory, particularly for numbers and factual information and retains high-level analytical and problem-solving skills. These strengths are consistent with her previous academic and professional achievements.

However, despite these cognitive strengths, Luke experiences significant executive functioning challenges in the context of emotional distress and fatigue. Her ability to initiate tasks, prioritise activities, sequence steps and sustain effort is highly variable and dependent on her emotional state. When emotionally overwhelmed, cognitive energy is rapidly depleted, resulting in reduced capacity to manage daily responsibilities.

This executive load affects Luke’s ability to maintain routines, complete personal care tasks, prepare meals, manage household responsibilities and engage in community activities consistently. Tasks that require planning, follow-through, or interaction with others are particularly demanding and are often delayed or avoided until they become urgent or reach crisis point.

Importantly, these difficulties do not reflect a lack of skill, insight or motivation. Rather, they reflect a reduced capacity to apply cognitive skills consistently in the context of ongoing emotional distress, sensory overload, sleep disruption and absence of external supports. Without structured assistance, Luke’s executive resources are insufficient to sustain safe and effective daily functioning over time.

## Impact of Sleep and Fatigue on Psychosocial Functioning {#impact-of-sleep-and-fatigue-on-psychosocial-functioning}

Sleep disturbance is a significant contributing factor to Luke’s psychosocial difficulties. She reports persistent difficulty initiating sleep, often remaining awake until the early hours of the morning due to inability to unwind and ongoing rumination about the day. Once asleep, she experiences frequent nightmares and fragmented sleep, resulting in poor sleep quality.

As a result, Luke often experiences significant daytime fatigue, reduced emotional tolerance and diminished cognitive stamina. On some days, exhaustion leads to daytime napping, which further disrupts sleep-wake cycles. Chronic fatigue exacerbates emotional dysregulation, reduces coping capacity and increases vulnerability to distress and crisis escalation.

Overall, Luke’s mental health and psychosocial functioning are characterised by high variability, limited resilience under stress and a reliance on crisis-based services in the absence of adequate daily supports. While she retains significant strengths in cognition and insight, her ability to translate these strengths into functional participation is compromised by emotional dysregulation, executive fatigue, sensory processing challenges, sleep disruption and complete absence of informal support.

Without consistent, skilled support particularly during evenings and weekends, Luke remains at high risk of ongoing crisis escalation, repeated hospital presentations and further functional decline. Proactive, community-based supports are essential to stabilise her psychosocial functioning, reduce reliance on acute services and enable gradual rebuilding of routine, participation and vocational capacity.

## WHODAS 2.0  {#whodas-2.0}

The World Health Organisation Disability Assessment Schedule 2.0 (WHODAS 2.0) is a standardised measure assessing disability across six functional domains: cognition, mobility, self-care, getting along with people, life activities and participation. It is recognised by the NDIS as a best-practice tool for evaluating functional impairment related to psychosocial, cognitive and neurodevelopmental disability. Raw and domain-weighted scores reflect the severity of functional impact, with higher scores indicating greater levels of disability.

Luke’s WHODAS 2.0 results indicate an overall moderate level of functional disability, with extreme restrictions evident in getting along with people and participation, reflecting profound difficulty engaging safely and consistently in interpersonal relationships and community life. Her results are as follows:

* **Cognition: 20/100 (20%)** This score reflects mild functional difficulty in cognitive functioning. Luke demonstrates intact memory, attention and problem-solving abilities when emotionally regulated. However, there is marked variability within this domain. She experiences significant difficulty initiating and maintaining conversations, particularly when overwhelmed, which impacts her ability to communicate needs, engage with services and participate effectively in social or professional contexts.  
* **Mobility: 25/100 (25%)** This score indicates mild functional difficulty in mobility-related activities. Luke is physically able to mobilise independently however, her capacity to leave the home is significantly impacted by emotional distress, sensory overload and anxiety. At times, she reports severe difficulty getting out of the home, reflecting psychosocial and environmental barriers rather than physical limitation.  
* **Self-Care: 40/100 (40%)** Luke’s score reflects moderate difficulty maintaining consistent self-care routines. She experiences challenges with regular showering, dressing and eating due to emotional exhaustion, prioritisation difficulties and depleted cognitive energy. These challenges are not related to lack of skill but rather reduced capacity to sustain routines independently, resulting in inconsistent self-care and increased vulnerability during periods of distress.  
* **Getting Along with People: 100/100 (100%)** This score indicates extreme difficulty in interpersonal functioning. Luke experiences profound challenges dealing with unfamiliar people, maintaining relationships and forming new social connections. Communication difficulties, emotional dysregulation and sensory overload significantly limit her ability to engage with others safely and consistently. This domain represents one of the most significant areas of functional impact and substantially affects her ability to access services and participate in the community without support.  
* **Life Activities: 50/100 (50%)** This score reflects moderate difficulty managing life activities, including household responsibilities and daily tasks. Luke demonstrates difficulty initiating, organising and completing domestic activities such as meal preparation, laundry and routine tasks. Tasks requiring planning, sequencing, time management or sustained effort are particularly compromised, resulting in inconsistent functioning and reliance on external support to maintain daily living.  
* **Participation: 96/100 (96%)** This score reflects extreme restriction in Luke’s participation in broader life situations. She experiences significant barriers to community involvement, social participation and meaningful engagement due to emotional distress, sensory sensitivities, financial strain and lack of informal support. Her health condition has a pervasive emotional impact and consumes a substantial amount of her time and energy, significantly limiting her capacity to participate independently in society.

Overall, Luke’s WHODAS 2.0 profile demonstrates a clinically significant level of functional disability, with the greatest impact evident in interpersonal functioning and participation, alongside moderate difficulty in self-care and life activities. These findings are consistent with Luke’s reported daily functioning and strongly support the need for ongoing, structured and skilled supports to assist with emotional regulation, routine management, community engagement and reduction of crisis-driven service use. Without sustained intervention, Luke remains at high risk of ongoing functional instability and continued reliance on acute hospital-based supports.

## Adolescent/Adult Sensory Profile {#adolescent/adult-sensory-profile}

The Adolescent/Adult Sensory Profile is a standardised self-report questionnaire used to evaluate an individual’s sensory processing patterns in everyday environments. It measures behavioural responses to sensory input across sensory processing quadrants and domains including auditory, visual, tactile, movement and activity level. Scores are compared with same-aged peers, with classifications indicating typical responses or increased sensory sensitivity, avoidance or reduced sensory seeking. The tool assists clinicians in identifying sensory modulation differences that may impact emotional regulation, attention and participation in daily activities.

Luke’s results on the Adolescent/Adult Sensory Profile revealed the following:

**Sensory Sensitivity:** Much more than most people (approximately ≥ 84th-98th percentile)  
Luke demonstrates a low neurological threshold for sensory input, meaning she responds quickly and intensely to sensory stimuli. She is highly sensitive to auditory and visual input and becomes easily overwhelmed by noise, bright lighting, movement and busy environments. This heightened sensory sensitivity contributes to emotional overload, fatigue, withdrawal and difficulty sustaining engagement in daily tasks and community settings.

**Sensation Avoiding:** Much more than most people (approximately ≥ 84th-98th percentile)  
Luke actively avoids sensory input in order to manage overwhelm. She limits exposure to noise, crowds and visual stimulation by remaining at home, keeping curtains closed and avoiding busy or unpredictable environments. While these avoidance strategies reduce immediate distress, they significantly restrict her participation in community activities, appointments and social engagement.

**Sensation Seeking:** Much less than most people (approximately ≤ 16th percentile)  
Luke demonstrates markedly reduced sensory-seeking behaviours. She does not naturally seek out sensory input to support regulation and instead relies heavily on avoidance-based strategies. This reduced sensory seeking limits opportunities for movement, exploration and restorative sensory experiences, contributing to isolation, reduced activity levels and difficulty accessing enjoyable or meaningful occupations.

**Low Registration:** Similar to most people (approximately 16th-84th percentile)  
Luke’s responses in the Low Registration quadrant fall within the typical range when compared to same-aged peers. This indicates that her primary sensory challenges relate to heightened sensitivity and active avoidance rather than reduced awareness of sensory input.

**Auditory Processing** (consistent with Sensory Sensitivity and Sensation Avoiding profiles \- elevated relative to same-aged peers)  
Luke demonstrates significant auditory sensitivity, consistent with her classification in the Sensory Sensitivity and Sensation Avoiding quadrants (both in the much more than most people range). She is highly responsive to background noise, sudden sounds and competing auditory input. These auditory demands significantly interfere with her ability to concentrate, regulate emotions and remain in shared or public environments. Auditory overload frequently leads to withdrawal, avoidance and escalation of distress, particularly in community and service-based settings.

**Visual Processing** (consistent with Sensory Sensitivity profile \- elevated relative to same-aged peers)  
Luke experiences marked visual overstimulation, consistent with her Sensory Sensitivity classification (much more than most people). Bright lighting, visual clutter and movement rapidly lead to fatigue, reduced attention and emotional overload. She manages this by controlling light exposure in her home environment, such as keeping curtains closed, however, this strategy further limits her ability to engage in daily and community activities.

**Touch and Personal Space** (consistent with Sensory Sensitivity and Sensation Avoiding profiles – elevated relative to same-aged peers)  
Luke demonstrates heightened sensitivity to tactile input and close physical proximity. Crowded environments, unexpected touch or reduced personal space increase distress and contribute to avoidance of public transport, waiting areas and social settings. These responses are consistent with her elevated Sensory Sensitivity and Sensation Avoiding profiles and significantly restrict her capacity to participate comfortably in community environments.

**Movement Processing** (variable sensory responses \- movement input may increase anxiety rather than regulate)  
Luke reports discomfort with certain movement-related sensory input, including fear of heights and unease with unfamiliar movement experiences. While physically capable, movement-based sensory input does not function as a regulating strategy for her and may instead increase anxiety and dysregulation, particularly in unfamiliar or unpredictable environments. This contributes to avoidance of certain community activities and environments that involve movement demands.

**Social-Emotional and Attentional Responses Associated with Sensory Processing:**  
Luke demonstrates heightened social-emotional and attentional responses to sensory input, consistent with elevated Sensory Sensitivity and Sensation Avoiding profiles (approximately ≥ 84th percentile). She experiences rapid emotional escalation, withdrawal and difficulty sustaining attention when overwhelmed. She pays significant attention to sensory cues in her environment, making it difficult to filter irrelevant input and maintain focus during tasks or interactions.

Overall, Luke’s sensory profile demonstrates a pattern of significantly heightened sensory sensitivity and sensory avoidance, with reduced sensory-seeking behaviours. These sensory modulation differences substantially impact her emotional regulation, attention and ability to participate consistently in daily activities. Luke’s capacity to remain regulated is highly dependent on predictable environments, reduced sensory input and proactive sensory supports. Without these supports, she is at increased risk of sensory overload, emotional dysregulation, fatigue and withdrawal from community participation.

Importantly, Luke’s current functional presentation does not reflect a permanent loss of skill or capability. Rather, it reflects a mismatch between her level of need and the supports currently available to her. With appropriate, consistent and skilled supports in place, Luke has a strong capacity to regain and sustain higher levels of functional participation, including improved independence in daily living, increased community engagement and gradual re-entry into vocational roles. Her history of high-level functioning indicates significant potential for recovery and capacity building when supports are adequately matched to her needs.

The absence of informal supports significantly increases Luke’s vulnerability. Living alone with no family or friends available to provide assistance or early intervention means that fluctuations in functioning often escalate before support is accessed. As a result, Luke currently relies heavily on crisis-based systems, including frequent emergency department presentations and hospital admissions. Evenings and weekends remain particularly high-risk periods due to reduced structure, accumulated fatigue and lack of available supports.

Overall, without proactive, skilled and adequately funded community-based supports, Luke remains at high risk of ongoing crisis escalation, continued hospital reliance and further functional instability. Conversely, with appropriate supports in place, she is well positioned to build capacity, stabilise functioning and work meaningfully toward long-term goals such as stable housing, employment and sustained community participation.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support  independence, safety, and wellbeing:

**1\. Support Worker Assistance**

**Recommended: 20 hours per week (4 hours per day x 5 days, including capacity for evenings/weekends)**

It is strongly recommended that Luke receive increased support worker assistance of approximately 4 hours per day, 5 days per week, with access to evening and weekend support. Luke would benefit from 3 days during the week and 2 days on the weekend for support. 

Luke requires consistent, hands-on support to manage daily living tasks, emotional regulation and community access. Current funding of 3 hours per week is significantly insufficient and does not reflect her level of functional need.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Support with daily routines including personal care, meal preparation and eating  
* Assistance with shopping, laundry and household tasks  
* Emotional regulation support during periods of distress to prevent escalation  
* Support during evenings and weekends, identified as high-risk times  
* Community access and accompaniment to appointments and essential activities

Adequate support worker hours are essential to reduce crisis presentations and enable Luke to remain safely in the community.

**2\. Occupational Therapy**

**Recommended: 67 hours per year (approximately 1 hour per week \+ an additional 15 hours for report writing)**

Ongoing occupational therapy is recommended to support capacity building, routine development and long-term functional stability.

Recommended areas of focus include:

* Establishment of sustainable daily routines for personal care, eating and sleep  
* Executive functioning strategies to support task initiation, prioritisation and follow-through  
* Sensory regulation strategies and environmental modifications to reduce overload  
* Development of daily living skills to support independence and safety  
* Gradual re-engagement in meaningful activities and preparation for return to work  
* Support with community participation and travel planning

Occupational therapy will provide the structured, skill-based intervention required to translate Luke’s cognitive strengths into functional participation.

**3\. Speech Pathology**

**Recommended: 26-40 hours per year (fortnightly to weekly, depending on capacity)**

Speech pathology intervention is recommended to support Luke’s functional communication and social interaction skills.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Pragmatic communication skills, including tone, directness and conversational flow  
* Interpretation of social cues and others’ intentions  
* Conflict resolution and repair strategies  
* Communication strategies during periods of emotional overload or shutdown  
* Self-advocacy and clearer expression of needs

Improved communication skills will support Luke’s ability to engage with services, participate in the community and maintain future workplace relationships.

**4\. Psychology**

**Recommended: 40-52 hours per year (weekly or fortnightly, transitioning as needed)**

Psychology intervention is recommended to support Luke’s long-term emotional regulation, maintenance of therapeutic progress and ongoing functional stability once current time-limited mental health treatment concludes.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Maintenance and consolidation of emotional regulation and coping strategies developed during current treatment  
* Trauma-informed therapy to support ongoing recovery and reduce vulnerability to relapse  
* Development of relapse prevention strategies and early intervention during periods of increased stress  
* Support to reduce reliance on crisis-based services and emergency department presentations  
* Integration of therapeutic strategies into daily routines, community participation and future vocational engagement

Ongoing psychology will support Luke to sustain progress, maintain emotional stability and reduce the risk of future crisis escalation as external mental health supports transition or conclude.

**5\. Mental Health Nursing**

**Recommended: 26-52 hours per year (fortnightly to weekly, flexible scheduling)**

Access to mental health nursing support is recommended to provide skilled, consistent assistance during periods of emotional distress.

Luke experiences frequent emotional escalation and crisis presentations. Support from experienced mental health professionals can assist with early intervention, emotional containment and implementation of coping strategies, reducing reliance on hospital-based care.

**6\. Art Therapy**

**Recommended: 26 hours per year (fortnightly sessions)**

Art therapy is recommended as a non-verbal therapeutic intervention, particularly given Luke’s alexithymia and history of communication shutdown during distress.

Luke has previously engaged in art therapy and reported it as beneficial. Art therapy provides a safe, non-verbal outlet for emotional processing and regulation and complements other therapeutic interventions.

**7\. Crisis Support / Hospital Avoidance**

**Recommended: 40-60 hours per year (Due to episodic usage required)** 

Funding for crisis support is strongly recommended to allow Luke to access additional in-home or community-based support during periods of escalation, as an alternative to emergency department presentations and hospital admission.

Proactive crisis support would enable Luke to remain in her home environment with appropriate support, reduce trauma associated with repeated hospitalisation and improve long-term functional outcomes.

**8\. Employment / Workplace Support**

**Recommended: 52 hours per year (approximately 1 hour per week)**

Ongoing employment support is recommended to assist Luke to return to work gradually and sustainably.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Workplace readiness and pacing  
* Support with workplace communication and relationships  
* Conflict resolution and advocacy  
* Ongoing support beyond short-term job placement

Given Luke’s history of high-level professional employment and the complexity of her needs, employment support should be ongoing rather than time-limited.

**9\. Behaviour Support Practitioner (Positive Behaviour Support)**

**Recommended: 30-40 hours per year**

Assessment and intervention from a Behaviour Support Practitioner (BSP) is strongly recommended to support Luke to reduce reliance on emergency services and develop alternative strategies for managing periods of acute emotional distress.

Luke experiences ongoing self-harm and suicidal behaviours during periods of escalation, which currently result in frequent emergency department presentations. These responses are distressing and at times, retraumatising, and do not provide sustainable strategies for long-term emotional regulation. Behaviour support involvement would provide a preventative, capacity-building approach focused on understanding triggers, developing safer coping strategies and strengthening support responses in the community.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Comprehensive behavioural assessment to identify triggers, maintaining factors and early warning signs of escalation  
* Development of proactive, preventative strategies to support emotional regulation  
* Creation of practical, individualised strategies to reduce reliance on emergency services  
* Training and guidance for regular support workers to respond effectively and safely during periods of distress  
* Collaboration with existing mental health and allied health supports to ensure consistency of approach

Positive Behaviour Support will support Luke to remain safely in the community, reduce crisis-driven service use and improve overall quality of life.

**10\. Exercise Physiology / Therapeutic Personal Training**

**Recommended: 26-40 hours per year (fortnightly to weekly initially)**

Funding for exercise physiology or appropriately qualified personal training is recommended to support Luke’s emotional regulation, mental health and routine development.

Luke has a background as an elite-level gymnast and identifies physical activity as a previously effective strategy for mood regulation and emotional wellbeing. Due to deterioration in mental health over recent years, regular physical activity has significantly reduced and is no longer prioritised, despite clear benefits. 

Recommended focus areas include:

* Re-establishing regular, structured physical activity in a supportive and graded manner  
* Supporting emotional regulation, stress reduction and mood stabilisation  
* Building routine, motivation and confidence through guided movement  
* Ensuring physical activity is approached therapeutically rather than performance-focused

Exercise-based supports should be delivered by a provider experienced in working with individuals with mental health and psychosocial disability and integrated with Luke’s broader support plan.

**11\. Support Coordination**

**Recommended: 52-78 hours per year**

Ongoing support coordination is strongly recommended to assist Luke to navigate, implement and maintain a complex support network.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Coordination of multiple providers across psychosocial and capacity-building supports  
* Assistance with service engagement and scheduling  
* Support with housing transition requirements  
* Ongoing monitoring and adjustment of supports

Given the complexity of Luke’s presentation and lack of informal supports, support coordination is essential to ensure services are effective, integrated and sustainable.
