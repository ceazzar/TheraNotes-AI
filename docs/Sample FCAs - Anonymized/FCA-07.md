# **Report Overview** {#report-overview}

# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

James is a neurodivergent young person with a confirmed diagnosis of Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) Level 2 and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). These conditions significantly influence his cognitive, sensory and social functioning. James experiences challenges with memory, attention, organisational skills and emotional regulation, all of which impact his ability to consistently participate in daily routines without support. 

His ADHD contributes to distractibility and a constant need for stimulation in order to maintain focus, while his autistic profile presents as sensory sensitivities, difficulties with interpreting complex or overwhelming environments and challenges with flexible thinking. James reports a long-standing difficulty with spatial awareness, often bumping into objects or forgetting where he is going. 

Although he is otherwise physically healthy, his neurodevelopmental presentation results in notable fluctuations in capacity across home, school, and community environments.

## Social Background {#social-background}

James divides his time between both parents’ homes, spending approximately 60% of the week with his mother in Malvern and the remaining 40% with his father in Ringwood. This arrangement is stable, and both households provide supportive environments. James is one of four siblings and is the second youngest. He generally gets along well with his sisters and describes these relationships as positive most of the time. 

Socially, James has a small group of close friends at school noting three peers with whom he feels most comfortable. While James enjoys social connection and seeks out companionship, he also finds busy, noisy or unpredictable social environments overwhelming, which can limit his participation. He often prefers small, familiar groups where communication is predictable and less demanding. James’s social world is meaningful to him, yet navigating it requires significant emotional and sensory effort and he frequently withdraws when overstimulated.

## Formal Supports {#formal-supports}

James has accessed a range of formal supports throughout his life, though many of these have been intermittent. He previously engaged with speech pathology through his school, which ceased approximately three years ago when he changed schools. He also attended psychology fortnightly but is not currently engaged, as he reports his needs in this area are not as prominent at present. 

James currently receives regular support from a personal trainer twice a week. These sessions were initially introduced following a history of bullying and they now play an important role in building James’s confidence, physical health and emotional regulation through structured movement. 

At this stage, James does not have regular allied health involvement beyond personal training, though there is emerging need for occupational therapy and possibly support worker assistance to build independence as he approaches adolescence and greater responsibility.

## Informal Supports  {#informal-supports}

James’s primary informal supports are his mother and father, who provide daily structure, transport, reminders and emotional support across both households. They assist James with organising routines, prompting him to complete hygiene tasks, managing his medication, ensuring he eats regularly and supervising community activities that may be overwhelming. James also receives day-to-day social support from his siblings and his close group of school friends, who help him feel connected and included. 

However, while his informal support network is strong, much of James’s functional independence relies on the consistent availability and oversight of his parents. He depends heavily on adult prompting to remember essential tasks, interpret sensory experiences, manage his schedule and stay safe in community environments. 

## Home Environment  {#home-environment}

James alternates between two familiar home environments, both of which provide stability and routine, although each presents different functional demands. Neither home has physical barriers that impact mobility or access however, sensory aspects of the environment can significantly influence James’s comfort and behaviour. James experiences heightened sensitivity to loud noises, yelling or competing sounds within the home, which trigger sensory overload and emotional dysregulation. He reports that sudden noise whether from others or even from himself can be distressing and contribute to agitation or withdrawal. 

Both homes offer structured expectations around chores and routines, though James often requires prompting to complete tasks such as cleaning, organising his bedroom or participating in household responsibilities. He also navigates both households using the tram system for school and community activities when predictable, though he avoids unfamiliar routes due to fear of becoming lost. 

Overall, James’s home environments are supportive, but his sensory sensitivities, executive functioning challenges and fluctuating routines create functional barriers that impact independence across everyday tasks.

# **Part B: Mental Health And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-health-and-psychosocial-functioning}

James’s psychosocial and cognitive functioning is significantly influenced by the combined impact of Autism Spectrum Disorder Level 2 and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. These neurodevelopmental differences shape the way he processes information, regulates emotions, responds to sensory input, interprets social cues and manages everyday demands. While James presents as friendly, bright and engaging, his internal experience of the world is often fast paced, overwhelming and difficult to organise, resulting in fluctuating levels of independence and participation across home, school and community settings.

## Emotional Regulation {#emotional-regulation}

James experiences considerable difficulty with emotional regulation, particularly in the context of sensory overwhelm, high-stimulation environments or when faced with demands he perceives as challenging. Loud noises and yelling whether occurring around him or coming from himself unintentionally are key triggers for dysregulation. He becomes easily overwhelmed in environments with competing sensory input such as crowded classrooms, busy shopping centres or noisy family settings. In these moments, James’s emotional responses escalate and he may become agitated, stressed, withdrawn or disorganised in his behaviour.

Transitions and time pressures are also areas of vulnerability for James. Mornings are described as chaotic and difficult to manage, with James often leaving the most demanding tasks until the last minute, which heightens his stress and reduces his ability to regulate effectively. When overloaded emotionally, James may lose the capacity to communicate clearly, remember basic instructions or follow through on tasks. His coping strategies are still developing, and he relies heavily on predictable routines and external supports to maintain emotional stability.

## Psychosocial Functioning {#psychosocial-functioning}

Socially, James is motivated to connect with others and maintains a small group of close friends with whom he feels comfortable. These friendships provide a sense of belonging and consistency. However, navigating broader social environments can be challenging for him due to sensory demands, communication differences and difficulty interpreting unpredictable social cues. James prefers interactions that are familiar, structured and low-stimulation. 

James’s history of bullying has shaped his self-confidence and emotional resilience. Although he has supportive friendships now, past experiences continue to influence how he perceives himself in social contexts, contributing at times to anxiety, self-consciousness and a tendency to monitor his behaviour closely. He is highly sensitive to interpersonal dynamics and may misinterpret tone or intention, particularly when under stress. While James values connection, the cognitive and sensory load required to maintain relationships often results in fatigue, meaning he requires downtime to recover after social engagement.

## Cognitive Functioning & Executive Skills {#cognitive-functioning-&-executive-skills}

James displays significant executive functioning challenges, which are characteristic of both ASD and ADHD. He experiences frequent difficulties with working memory, task initiation, planning, organisation, sequencing and sustaining attention. James describes his memory as “terrible” unless he feels motivated or the information is personally rewarding. As a result, he often forgets routine tasks such as brushing his teeth, taking medication or eating lunch. His distractibility leads to unfinished tasks, lost belongings and difficulty transitioning between activities.

Academically, James struggles with maintaining attention during class, particularly when the content is unstimulating or requires sustained mental effort. He benefits from external structures such as guided study periods at school, as he is unlikely to complete homework independently at home without prompting. He experiences pronounced stress around assessments, especially when deadlines approach or group work is required. In these contexts, the combined pressure of expectations, time demands and social coordination can lead to overwhelm, reduced performance or disengagement.

This contributes to morning dysregulation, reduced attention and increased irritability throughout the day. Sleep impairment is a central factor that exacerbates James’s cognitive load, sensory sensitivity and overall ability to cope with daily routines.

## WHODAS-Child {#whodas-child}

The World Health Organisation Disability Assessment Schedule – Children and Youth version (WHODAS-Child) is a standardised measure assessing disability across six functional domains: cognition, mobility, self-care, getting along with people, life activities and participation. It is recognised by the NDIS as a best-practice tool for evaluating functional impairment related to neurodevelopmental, cognitive and psychosocial disability. Raw and domain-weighted scores reflect the severity of functional impact, with higher scores indicating greater levels of disability.

James’s WHODAS-Child results indicate an overall moderate level of functional impairment, with severe restrictions evident in life activities and participation, reflecting substantial difficulty managing daily responsibilities and sustaining consistent engagement across home, school and community environments. His scores are as follows:

* **Cognition: 46/100 (46%)** This score reflects moderate impairment in James’s cognitive functioning, including attention, memory, learning and problem-solving. He experiences difficulty sustaining concentration, remembering important tasks, processing information efficiently and managing everyday problems independently. These challenges significantly affect his ability to complete schoolwork, follow instructions and organise tasks without structured support, prompting and external scaffolding.  
* **Mobility: 50/100 (50%)** This score indicates moderate functional impairment in mobility-related activities. While James is physically able to ambulate independently, his functional mobility is impacted by reduced body awareness, distractibility and difficulty sustaining physical effort. These challenges can affect his safety awareness and efficiency when navigating school and community environments, particularly in busy or overstimulating settings.  
* **Self-Care: 44/100 (44%)** James’s score reflects moderate impairment in self-care activities. He experiences difficulty maintaining consistency with personal hygiene, dressing routines and staying safe when alone. Reduced working memory, task initiation difficulties and inconsistent awareness of daily needs contribute to reliance on parental prompting and supervision to ensure basic self-care tasks are completed.  
* **Getting Along with People: 45/100 (45%)** This score indicates moderate impairment in interpersonal functioning. James experiences difficulty interpreting social cues, managing emotional responses and maintaining relationships, particularly in unfamiliar or demanding social situations. Communication challenges, emotional dysregulation and sensory overload further impact his ability to sustain positive interactions without support.  
* **Life Activities: 75/100 (75%)** This score indicates severe impairment in James’s ability to manage life activities, including household responsibilities and school-related tasks. He demonstrates significant difficulty initiating, organising and completing chores, homework and daily expectations. Tasks requiring planning, sequencing, time management or sustained effort are particularly compromised, resulting in a high level of dependence on adult support and supervision to maintain daily functioning.  
* **Participation: 41/100 (41%)** This score reflects severe restriction in James’s participation in broader life situations. While James can engage in preferred activities independently, his overall participation is limited by sensory sensitivities, emotional dysregulation, cognitive fatigue and reliance on family support. The WHODAS-Child notes variability within this domain, with high dependence on family members to manage the impact of his health condition, alongside relative strengths in engaging in enjoyable activities when environments are structured and supportive.

Overall, James’s WHODAS-Child profile demonstrates a clinically significant level of functional disability, with the greatest impact evident in life activities and participation, alongside moderate impairment across cognition, mobility, self-care and interpersonal functioning. These findings are consistent with James’s reported daily functioning and strongly support the need for ongoing, structured supports to assist with routine management, skill development, community participation and the gradual building of independence. Without sustained intervention, James remains at risk of ongoing functional limitations and increasing reliance on family supports as demands increase with age.

## Child Sensory Profile  {#child-sensory-profile}

The Child Sensory Profile 2 (SP2) is a standardised caregiver questionnaire used to evaluate a child’s sensory processing patterns in everyday environments. It measures behavioural responses to sensory input across auditory, visual, tactile, movement, body position, and oral sensory domains. Scores are compared with same-aged peers, with higher percentile ranges indicating greater sensory sensitivity, avoidance or seeking behaviours. The SP2 assists clinicians in identifying sensory modulation differences that may impact attention, emotional regulation, behaviour and participation in daily activities.

James’s results on the Child Sensory Profile 2 revealed the following:

**Auditory Processing:** More than others (86th-96th percentile)  
James demonstrates heightened responsiveness to auditory input and is particularly sensitive to loud, unexpected or competing sounds. Background noise such as televisions, music, fans or classroom noise significantly interferes with his ability to concentrate and complete tasks. He may appear distracted, tune out spoken information or become unproductive in noisy environments. These auditory sensitivities contribute to increased cognitive load, irritability and reduced functional engagement, particularly in busy school or home settings.

**Visual Processing:** Much more than others (99th percentile)  
James demonstrates marked visual sensitivity and heightened attention to visual stimuli. He is more bothered by bright lights than his peers and shows a strong preference for visually engaging details, patterns and movement in his environment. Visually cluttered or brightly lit spaces can quickly become overwhelming, contributing to fatigue, distraction and difficulty sustaining focus. James also requires assistance locating objects that are obvious to others, reflecting challenges filtering visual information in complex environments.

**Touch Processing:** More than others (88th-96th percentile)  
James shows increased sensitivity to tactile input and may experience distress or irritation in response to touch, grooming tasks or close physical proximity to others. He can become anxious in crowded spaces and may react emotionally when touched unexpectedly. At the same time, James also displays tactile seeking behaviours, such as touching objects, surfaces or people to gain sensory input. This combination reflects difficulty modulating tactile information and contributes to emotional dysregulation in unpredictable environments.

**Movement Processing:** More than others (86th-96th percentile)  
James exhibits increased responsiveness to movement and demonstrates strong sensory-seeking behaviours related to movement. He frequently pursues movement to the point that it interferes with daily routines, including fidgeting, rocking and difficulty remaining seated. He may also take movement-related risks without full awareness of safety. While movement is a critical regulatory strategy for James, excessive or unstructured movement can contribute to accidents, fatigue and reduced task engagement.

**Body Position Processing:** Much more than others (97th-99th percentile)  
James shows significant differences in proprioceptive processing, with reduced awareness of body position and physical effort. He may appear clumsy, bump into objects, lose balance unexpectedly or demonstrate low muscle tone and fatigue when maintaining posture. James often seeks deep pressure input, such as leaning on furniture, draping himself over objects or people or using heavy blankets for sleep. These proprioceptive differences impact his coordination, endurance and physical regulation throughout the day.

**Oral Sensory Processing:** Typical range  
James’s oral sensory processing falls within the typical range when compared to same-aged peers. While he displays some preferences around tastes, textures and oral input, these behaviours are not significantly elevated and do not currently present as a primary area of sensory difficulty. Oral sensory input does not appear to be a major driver of his regulation or dysregulation.

**Social-Emotional and Attentional Responses Associated with Sensory Processing:**  
James demonstrates ‘more than others’ to ‘much more than others’ responses in both social-emotional and attentional domains. He experiences strong emotional reactions when overwhelmed by sensory input and may become frustrated, distressed or withdrawn when unable to complete tasks or when routines change unexpectedly. His attentional responses indicate that he pays significantly more attention to sensory cues in the environment than peers, making it difficult for him to filter irrelevant information. This contributes to distractibility, getting lost easily, difficulty sustaining focus and heightened emotional responses under cognitive or sensory load.

Overall, James’s sensory profile demonstrates a complex pattern of heightened sensory sensitivity, sensory seeking and reduced sensory registration, particularly across auditory, visual, tactile, movement and proprioceptive systems. These sensory modulation differences significantly affect his attention, emotional regulation, coordination and ability to participate consistently in daily activities. James’s capacity to remain regulated is highly dependent on predictable routines, structured environments, access to movement and deep pressure input and proactive sensory supports. Without these supports, he is at increased risk of sensory overload, emotional dysregulation, fatigue and reduced functional participation across home, school and community settings.

# **Part E: Summary & Recommendations** {#part-e:-summary-&-recommendations}

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

James is a capable, personable and engaging young person whose functional capacity is significantly impacted by the combined effects of Autism Spectrum Disorder Level 2 and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. While James demonstrates clear strengths in areas such as cooking, independent mobility in familiar environments and maintaining social relationships with a small group of trusted peers, his overall functional presentation is marked by inconsistent performance across daily living, educational, domestic and community contexts. These inconsistencies are driven by ongoing difficulties with emotional regulation, executive functioning, sensory processing, sleep regulation and routine formation.

Results from standardised assessment tools strongly support these clinical observations. James’s WHODAS-Child profile identifies an overall moderate level of functional disability, with severe impairment in life activities and participation. These results reflect significant difficulty managing household responsibilities, schoolwork and daily expectations without substantial prompting and supervision. Tasks requiring planning, sequencing, time management, sustained effort or self-monitoring are particularly compromised. Moderate impairment across cognition, self-care, mobility and interpersonal functioning further highlights the pervasive nature of James’s challenges and their impact on independence.

James’s executive functioning impairments are a central contributor to his functional limitations. He experiences ongoing difficulties with working memory, task initiation, organisation, prioritisation, time awareness and follow-through, resulting in frequent exclusion of essential daily tasks such as eating lunch, brushing teeth, completing chores or taking medication. These difficulties persist despite adequate understanding of expectations and are not due to lack of skill or motivation, but rather reflect reduced capacity to independently manage and sustain routines. As a result, James relies heavily on parental prompting, supervision and external structure across both households to meet basic daily living requirements.

The Child Sensory Profile 2 further identifies significant sensory modulation differences, with heightened sensitivity and sensory-seeking behaviours across auditory, visual, tactile, movement and body position domains. James is particularly vulnerable to sensory overload in environments with loud noise, visual clutter, crowds or unpredictable movement. These sensory challenges contribute to emotional dysregulation, distractibility, reduced safety awareness and avoidance of certain community environments. Sensory overload also impacts James’s communication, with difficulty regulating tone and volume, increased reactivity and reduced ability to process verbal information when overstimulated.

James’s participation is further affected by sleep dysregulation, including delayed sleep onset, restless sleep and significant difficulty waking in the morning. This results in chronic fatigue, reduced cognitive stamina and increased emotional vulnerability, which compound his executive functioning and sensory regulation difficulties throughout the day. Fatigue significantly reduces James’s capacity to cope with demands, particularly during school days or periods of increased expectation.

Although James is able to complete many tasks independently when conditions are optimal such as when routines are predictable, environments are low-stimulation, and adult support is readily available his functional capacity remains highly variable and context-dependent. Without consistent environmental scaffolding, external reminders and adult supervision, James is unable to sustain safe, organised and effective participation across daily living, domestic tasks, educational demands and community activities. As developmental expectations increase, these functional impairments place James at risk of increasing dependence, emotional overwhelm, reduced participation and burnout.

Overall, James requires ongoing, structured and developmentally appropriate supports to address his executive functioning, sensory regulation, emotional wellbeing and participation needs. Without sustained intervention, he is likely to continue to experience significant barriers to independence and will remain reliant on family supports to manage daily functioning across multiple life domains.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support  independence, safety, and wellbeing:

**1\. Occupational Therapy**

Ongoing occupational therapy is recommended to address James’s executive functioning challenges, sensory needs, emotional regulation and daily living skills. OT will provide structured, capacity-building interventions to improve his independence across home, school and community settings.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Development of consistent routines for personal care, sleep, morning/evening transitions and medication adherence  
* Executive functioning supports including visual schedules, timers, task breakdowns, planners and organisational tools  
* Sensory regulation strategies for managing noise, overstimulation and emotional overwhelm  
* Capacity-building for domestic skills such as laundry, cleaning and room organisation  
* Mealtime routine development and interoceptive awareness strategies to support consistent eating  
* Community navigation skills, including safety, route planning and confidence in unfamiliar environments

OT involvement will help build James’s independence, reduce stress and improve consistency across routines and responsibilities.

**2\. Support Worker**

Support worker assistance is recommended to strengthen James’s independence, reduce reliance on family and support participation in the community.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Assistance with community access including transport practice, navigating unfamiliar places and accessing recreational activities  
* Support with shopping and multi-step tasks in busy environments that currently cause overwhelm  
* Domestic skill-building across both households including cleaning, organising, meal planning and laundry  
* Executive functioning coaching within daily tasks, such as reminders, task sequencing and setting up functional systems for organisation  
* Structured social and community engagement opportunities to promote confidence and independence

A support worker will provide consistent, hands-on skill development and reduce parental burden.

**3\. Exercise Physiology / Personal Training**

James benefits from structured movement to support emotional regulation, confidence and physical wellbeing. Additional or extended sessions will help maintain routine, fitness, and mental health.

Recommended Focus:

* Increase session length from 45 minutes to 1-1.15 hours  
* Provide one session per week at his father’s home to ensure equal access across households  
* Continue strength, coordination and confidence-building activities  
* Integrate body-awareness and sensory-regulation strategies through movement

These sessions provide a stable, predictable structure that supports James’s emotional and behavioural regulation.

**4\. Psychology** 

Re-engagement with psychology may be beneficial to support James’s emotional wellbeing, coping skills and resilience. While not an immediate priority, future therapy could support transitions into adolescence.

Possible Areas of Support:

* Emotional regulation strategies  
* Managing stress related to schoolwork and assessments  
* Addressing impacts of past bullying  
* Building communication confidence and social problem-solving skills

**5\. Support Coordination** 

Support Coordination is recommended to assist James and his family to effectively navigate and implement the range of supports required to meet his current and emerging needs. Given James’s complex neurodevelopmental profile, dual-household living arrangement and reliance on structured supports across multiple domains, coordination of services is essential to ensure consistency, reduce caregiver burden and optimise outcomes.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Assistance to identify, engage, and coordinate appropriate providers across occupational therapy, speech pathology, support work and exercise-based supports  
* Support to align strategies and routines across both parental households to promote consistency and reduce functional disruption  
* Assistance with service scheduling, communication between providers and monitoring of support effectiveness  
* Ongoing review and adjustment of supports as James’s functional needs and developmental expectations change

Support Coordination will assist in ensuring James’s supports are well-integrated, responsive and sustainable, while reducing the administrative and emotional load on his parents.

**6\. Speech Pathology**

Speech pathology intervention is recommended to support James’s social communication and pragmatic language skills, which are significantly impacted by ASD and contribute to difficulties with relationships, emotional regulation and participation.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Development of social cue awareness, including recognising non-verbal communication, tone and perspective-taking  
* Support with conversation management skills, such as turn-taking, topic maintenance and interpreting intent in social interactions  
* Explicit teaching of conflict resolution strategies, including expressing needs, negotiating disagreements and repairing relationships  
* Support to manage communication shutdowns during conflict or emotional overload, with strategies to remain engaged or re-enter conversations safely  
* Building emotional literacy and self-advocacy skills to support clearer communication of feelings and needs

Speech pathology will support James to communicate more effectively across home, school and community environments, reduce social misunderstandings and build confidence in managing interpersonal challenges.
