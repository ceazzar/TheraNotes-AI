# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Priya is a young adult with a complex psychosocial and neurodevelopmental presentation. She received a formal diagnosis of Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) Level 2/3 last year, following longstanding difficulties with communication, sensory regulation, social participation and flexible thinking. In addition to autism, Priya was diagnosed with Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) at approximately 18 years of age and Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) around the same time. She also reports the onset of significant agoraphobic symptoms around age 16, describing intense anxiety and discomfort when leaving the house or being in public spaces. These conditions are longstanding and have significantly shaped her development, relationships and functional capacity.

Priya experiences a high level of psychological distress and complex mental health symptoms. She reports frequent visual and auditory hallucinations, such as seeing people who are not there or hearing voices warning that “someone is coming” or that people are after her. These symptoms lead to pronounced hypervigilance and fear, for example needing to sleep fully under the covers because she believes someone may be in her room and repeatedly checking behind her when walking alone. Priya’s mental health diagnoses and associated symptoms significantly impact her emotional regulation, sleep, participation in daily activities and ability to engage safely in the community.

Priya also describes periods of manic or elevated mood characterised by intense energy, impulsive decision-making and hyperfixation. For example, she enrolled in a nursing course at TAFE at 3am during a manic episode and did not recall enrolling until contacted by the provider. During these elevated periods she may stay up late, undertake activities such as baking or rapidly commit to new jobs or projects. These episodes, combined with her underlying mood and trauma-related vulnerabilities, contribute to a pattern of rapid engagement followed by abrupt withdrawal, exhaustion and decompensation. Taken together, Priya’s diagnostic profile reflects a chronic psychosocial disability with significant functional impact across all major life domains.

## Social Background {#social-background}

Priya lives in her family home with her mother, two sisters, brother and grandfather. She is the eldest sibling, which has at times brought added expectations and emotional responsibility. The home is physically accessible, but the interpersonal environment is complex and often distressing for Priya. Her father left the family when she was around 13 years old, moving interstate and now visits approximately every two weeks. Priya finds these visits highly uncomfortable and reports that she “is not a fan” of seeing him, highlighting unresolved emotional pain and difficulty reconciling these relationships.

Priya reports that interactions with her mother frequently become a source of dysregulation rather than support. She notes that, for a period, her siblings didn’t like her because she was always trying to hurt herself, reflecting the significant impact her mental health crises have had on family dynamics. While she now describes her relationships with siblings as okay overall, she acknowledges that things have been challenging, particularly around times of repeated hospital admissions and suicide attempts.

Socially, Priya is markedly isolated outside the family. She reports that she does not really have any friends and finds it extremely difficult to trust others or form new relationships. Priya describes herself as someone who fights with everyone as she feels every emotion much more intensely than other people. This pattern, consistent with her BPD diagnosis, leads to a strong fear of making new relationships, as she worries that conflict, rejection or abandonment will inevitably follow.

The intermittent presence of her ex-partner provides some emotional contact and respite from the home environment, but this relationship is itself conflictual and not reliably supportive. # **Part B: Mental Health And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-health-and-psychosocial-functioning}

## Emotional Regulation  {#emotional-regulation}

Priya experiences significant and persistent difficulties with emotional regulation, which are central to her functional presentation. She describes her emotions as feeling more intense than other people’s, and this is reflected in her day-to-day responses to relatively small triggers. Minor events such as someone’s tone of voice, a look or a small change in plans can quickly escalate into overwhelming distress. When this occurs, Priya often moves very rapidly from feeling unsettled to yelling, crying, or withdrawing to her room, where she may experience intense shame and fear that others now hate her. These responses are consistent with her diagnosis of Borderline Personality Disorder and are compounded by trauma history and autistic sensitivity to social cues.

Priya reports having episodes anywhere from one to five times per week, depending on what is happening in her life. During these episodes, her emotional state, hallucinations and paranoia all intensify. She may become extremely fearful, feel convinced someone is in her room or following her, and struggle to think clearly or engage in basic tasks. Priya often requires significant time to recover after an episode. These events are exhausting, both physically and emotionally and leave her feeling drained and less able to cope with everyday demands in the days that follow.

Sleep disruption further exacerbates Priya’s regulation difficulties. She has great difficulty falling asleep, wakes frequently throughout the night and then sleeps much of the day. This contributes to chronic fatigue, decreased frustration tolerance and impaired concentration. Although she has been prescribed Olanzapine for several years, it no longer provides adequate benefit, and Priya continues to rely on strategies such as watching the same familiar television show and using a fan to create white noise. Taken together, the combination of intense emotions, frequent episodes, chronic sleep disturbance and hallucinations results in a very fragile baseline level of regulation, leaving Priya highly vulnerable to crisis.

Priya’s psychosocial functioning is significantly impacted by her mental health conditions, neurodivergence and trauma history. After disagreements or misunderstandings, she may withdraw, ruminate and experience intense guilt or shame, which can further reinforce avoidance of social contact. Over time this has led to a pattern of social withdrawal and isolation, with most of her days spent at home, in bed or in her room, outside of scheduled appointments. She rarely engages in community activities just for enjoyment and does not currently participate in clubs, groups or structured social environments.

Priya does experience periods of increased activity when she becomes hyperfixated on a particular interest or routine. These bursts of engagement provide short-term structure and pleasure, but are typically brief. When energy or mood drops, or when anxiety or hallucinations increase, these activities are quickly abandoned, and Priya returns to a pattern of inactivity and withdrawal. The combination of limited social support, interpersonal sensitivity and unstable routines significantly reduces her capacity to participate meaningfully in home, community and relational roles.

Hospital admissions and acute mental health crises have had a profound impact on Priya’s functioning over many years. The impact of these crises extends beyond Priya herself. Her family has had to cope with repeated emergency presentations and the practical and emotional consequences of hospital stays. ## WHODAS 2.0 {#whodas-2.0}

The adult WHODAS 2.0 is a 36-item standardised measure assessing disability across six functional domains: understanding and communicating, getting around, self-care, getting along with people, life activities and participation in society. It is recognised by the NDIS as a best-practice tool for evaluating functional impairment related to psychosocial and neurodevelopmental disability. The WHODAS provides a percentage of impairment for each domain, with 100% indicating the highest level of disability.

Priya’s results indicate severe to extreme impairment across nearly all domains, consistent with her psychosocial disability, trauma history and neurodevelopmental needs.

* **Understanding and Communicating:** Priya scored 17/24 (71%) disability, indicating severe impairment in her ability to process, retain and respond to information. She reported severe difficulty concentrating, remembering important information, analysing everyday problems and learning new tasks. These findings reflect her lived experience of forgetfulness, losing her train of thought, becoming overwhelmed by multi-step tasks and struggling to understand or organise verbal information when distressed. Her emotional dysregulation, trauma responses and autistic communication profile further reduce her ability to engage meaningfully in conversations, problem-solve independently or follow instructions without support.  
* **Getting Around:** Priya scored 6/20 (30%) disability, indicating mild functional impairment in this domain. While she has no physical limitations with balance, coordination or walking, her results reflect the psychological barriers that significantly restrict her ability to access the community safely. She reported severe difficulty standing for prolonged periods and leaving the house independently. Although movement within the home is rated as none, Priya’s agoraphobia, hypervigilance and paranoia severely limit her capacity to engage in mobility-related community activities without support. Her functional access to the community is therefore substantially impaired, despite intact physical mobility.  
* **Self-Care:** Priya scored 11/16 (69%) disability, representing severe impairment in her ability to complete personal care tasks. She identified major difficulties with showering, washing her body and managing eating routines. Eating was rated extreme, reflecting her pattern of forgetting to eat throughout the day and binge-eating at night. She requires frequent prompting from her mother to shower, brush her teeth and maintain hygiene, and she often experiences sensory discomfort, low motivation and overwhelm that prevent consistent completion of self-care tasks. These findings align closely with her daily functioning and high reliance on caregiver support.  
* **Getting Along with People:** Priya scored 15/20 (75%) disability, indicating severe impairment in social interaction. She reported severe difficulty dealing with people she does not know, maintaining friendships, managing conflict and reading social cues. Emotional intensity, rapid escalation when distressed and difficulty regulating tone or verbal expression all contribute to strained interpersonal relationships. Fear of rejection and past negative experiences lead Priya to avoid social situations, resulting in marked social isolation. These findings reflect her BPD-related sensitivity, trauma responses and autistic communication differences, all of which significantly affect her capacity to form and maintain supportive relationships.  
* **Life Activities:** Priya scored 19/20 (95%) disability, representing extreme functional impairment in domestic life activities. She experiences severe difficulty initiating, organising and completing household tasks such as cleaning, laundry, meal preparation and maintaining routines. She relies heavily on her mother for most domestic responsibilities and frequently becomes overwhelmed by basic tasks. Work and study items were not scored due to current non-participation, which is consistent with her longstanding inability to sustain education or employment due to repeated hospital admissions, episodes and anxiety. This domain reflects the profound impact of her psychosocial disability on daily independence.  
* **Participation in Society:** Priya scored 26/32 (81%) disability, indicating severe restrictions in community and societal participation. She experiences extreme emotional impact from her health conditions and reports longstanding difficulties engaging in social, community and civic activities. Barriers such as paranoia, hallucinations, agoraphobia, fatigue, emotional overwhelm and frequent hospital admissions severely restrict her ability to leave the house, attend appointments independently or engage in meaningful community roles. She also reported significant consequences for her family and substantial disruption to daily functioning, reflecting chronic and pervasive disability.

Priya scored 74/100 (74%) overall, placing her within the severe range of functional disability. All domains except physical mobility demonstrate severe to extreme impairment. Her results highlight major functional barriers in understanding and communicating, self-care, social interaction, life activities and community participation. These findings strongly align with her clinical presentation, psychosocial history and observed functioning, demonstrating the profound and enduring impact of her mental health conditions and neurodevelopmental disability on her independence, safety and ability to participate in daily life.

## BRIEF-A  {#brief-a}

The BRIEF-A is a standardised clinical measure that assesses an individual’s everyday executive functioning across nine domains, including inhibition, shifting, emotional control, self-monitoring, initiation, working memory, planning, organisation and task monitoring. It evaluates how cognitive and self-regulatory processes impact daily routines, independence and problem-solving. Higher frequency of ‘often’ responses indicates greater impairment.

Priya’s responses indicate clinically significant executive dysfunction across all domains, with the majority of items marked ‘often’, demonstrating pervasive difficulties with initiation, emotional regulation, cognitive flexibility, task completion and self-monitoring. These findings are consistent with her psychosocial disability, ASD, trauma history and functional presentation.

* **Inhibit:** Priya endorses frequent difficulty controlling impulses, with multiple ‘often’ responses to items such as making careless mistakes, saying things without thinking, acting impulsively and making decisions that lead to negative consequences. She also reports regular angry outbursts and emotional overreactions to minor triggers. These findings indicate marked impairment in behavioural inhibition, contributing to interpersonal conflict, risk behaviours and difficulty managing daily routines.

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Priya presents with a complex and enduring psychosocial disability that significantly impacts her ability to manage daily life tasks, regulate emotions, and participate meaningfully in home, social and community environments. Her difficulties occur across multiple functional domains and are consistent with her diagnoses of Autism Spectrum Disorder (Level 2/3), Borderline Personality Disorder, PTSD and agoraphobia.

Priya experiences severe emotional dysregulation, with rapid escalation in response to minor triggers, frequent episodes of distress and hallucinations, and high levels of anxiety and paranoia. These episodes occur multiple times per week and often prevent her from completing basic daily activities, engaging in relationships or leaving the house safely. Sleep disturbance, chronic fatigue and hypervigilance further reduce her functional capacity and resilience.

Her executive functioning is markedly impaired, with significant challenges in initiation, planning, organisation, working memory and task completion. Priya often forgets tasks mid-way, loses items, becomes overwhelmed by multi-step activities and requires prompting to begin or follow through with simple routines. These difficulties contribute to inconsistent self-care, poor household management and reduced independence.

Priya’s self-care and daily living skills are heavily reliant on her mother. She frequently forgets or avoids showering, brushes her teeth only when leaving the house, struggles with regular eating patterns and spends much of the day in bed in pyjamas. Domestic tasks such as cooking, cleaning, laundry and household organisation are rarely completed without direct assistance and her functioning deteriorates further during periods of emotional distress.

Her social and communication functioning is also significantly affected. Priya struggles to articulate her thoughts and becomes verbally blocked or mute when overwhelmed, and may escalate to yelling when unable to express herself. She misinterprets social cues, fears rejection and avoids new relationships, contributing to severe social isolation and dependence on a very limited support network.

Priya’s community participation is extremely restricted despite intact physical mobility. Agoraphobia, paranoia and fear of harm prevent her from travelling or attending appointments independently, requiring support workers or her mother for all outings. She does not engage in structured social, recreational or community activities and spends most days at home.

Her ability to engage in education and employment is currently absent. Priya has a history of short-lived work attempts, with attendance disrupted by anxiety, hospital admissions, episodes and difficulty coping with workplace demands. She is unable to meet the consistency, emotional regulation, communication or executive functioning requirements of any vocational or educational setting.

Overall, Priya’s functional impairments are severe, pervasive and longstanding, and are broadly reflected in her standardised assessment results, which indicate significant limitations in day-to-day activities, executive functioning and participation. These difficulties profoundly impact her independence, safety and ability to manage daily life, highlighting the need for sustained, high-level support across multiple domains.
