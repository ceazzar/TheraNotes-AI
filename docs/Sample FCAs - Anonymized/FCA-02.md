# **Part B: Mental Health And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-health-and-psychosocial-functioning}

Chloe has a formal diagnosis of Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD) and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). These conditions have been present since childhood and continue to impact his day-to-day functioning.

ODD is characterised by persistent patterns of oppositional, defiant, and occasionally hostile behaviour, particularly in response to perceived control, authority, or rigid expectations. Chloe reports that while these behaviours were more pronounced during his school years, they have since become more manageable with maturity and supportive relationships. He is not currently experiencing any significant behavioural outbursts however, his history indicates vulnerability to behavioural dysregulation during periods of stress, change, or when supports are not consistent. Chloe presents with insight and a willingness to engage cooperatively with trusted supports.

Whereas, ADHD continues to impact Chloe’s executive functioning, with key challenges noted in working memory, task initiation, planning, and organisation. These difficulties are particularly evident in situations that require following multi-step instructions, independently completing tasks, or managing time-sensitive routines such as employment or appointments. Chloe reports that he is no longer prescribed medication (previously took Ritalin during school years) and is not currently engaged in formal mental health supports.

Nonetheless, Chloe acknowledged that he may benefit from psychological support in the future, especially during periods of change or when facing increased stress.

Overall, Chloe presents as emotionally stable in his current living situation but remains vulnerable to functional stressors due to his underlying neurodevelopmental conditions. Ongoing support to structure his routine, reduce cognitive load, and gradually build adaptive coping strategies will be beneficial.

## **BRIEF-A** {#brief-a}

The BRIEF-A is a standardised assessment used to evaluate executive function in adults. It consists of 75 items and measures an individual's ability to manage and regulate goal-directed behaviour in everyday settings. The BRIEF-A assesses multiple executive function domains including inhibition, working memory, planning and organisation, initiation, emotional control, and task monitoring.

Scores are presented as T-scores, with higher scores indicating greater difficulty in that domain. A T-score of 65 or above is considered clinically significant, while scores between 60–64 are mildly elevated and may indicate emerging or situational challenges.

Chloe’s results on the BRIEF-A revealed the following:

* **Working Memory:** Chloe demonstrated a clinically elevated score in this domain. This indicates significant difficulty with holding and manipulating information in mind for short periods, which impacts his ability to follow multi-step instructions, retain details, and remain focused on tasks.  
* **Planning and Organisation:** Chloe scored in the elevated range for this domain, indicating difficulty with organising tasks and materials, and with planning ahead. These challenges may impact his ability to initiate and complete domestic tasks such as cooking, cleaning, or preparing for activities without support.  
* **Initiation:** Chloe scored in the mildly elevated range, reflecting some difficulty with independently starting tasks or generating ideas. He may require support to get started on daily routines or employment-related activities.  
* **Task Monitoring:** Chloe’s scores suggest reduced ability to self-evaluate performance during tasks, which may result in errors or incomplete activities without prompting or feedback.  
* **Inhibition and Emotional Control:** Chloe scored within normal limits in these areas, indicating he is generally able to manage impulses and regulate his emotional responses appropriately.

Overall, Chloe’s results indicate mild to moderate executive function challenges, primarily affecting his working memory, planning, and initiation. These findings are consistent with the functional challenges reported during assessment and reflect the impact of his ODD and ADHD on daily life.

These results support the need for:

* Ongoing support to structure daily tasks and routines  
* Assistance with learning and memory strategies  
* Employment and training support that reduces reliance on self-directed problem-solving or multitasking 

## **Vinelands-3 Domain Level** {#vinelands-3-domain-level}

The Vineland-3 Survey Interview Form was completed with Chloe to evaluate adaptive functioning across three domains \- Communication, Daily Living Skills, and Socialization and the overall Adaptive Behaviour Composite (ABC).

Chloe’s results fall in the moderately low range across domains:

* Communication Standard Score=83 which is the 13th percentile  
* Daily Living Skills Standard Score=85 which is the 16th percentile  
* Socialization Standard Score=85 which is the 16th percentile  
* An overall ABC score of 82 which is in the 12th percentile

This indicates adaptive functioning below that expected for his age and a broadly even profile with no marked relative strengths, suggesting global rather than restricted adaptive limitations.

* **Communication:** Chloe is likely to experience difficulty understanding and expressing information when tasks are multi-step, rapid, or unfamiliar. He may miss details, struggle to summarise key points, and find it harder to complete forms or follow complex instructions without support. Functional impact is most evident in appointments, training/education, and problem-solving in real time.

  Supports that can help include using plain language, one-to-two-step instructions, visual/written prompts, and repetition with opportunities to practise in context.

* **Daily Living Skills:** Scores suggest reduced independence with planning/sequencing new daily routines, managing time and appointments, meal preparation, and navigating transport/community tasks efficiently and safely. Performance may drop further under pain, fatigue, or stress.

  Supports that can help include predictable routines, checklists, phone or calendar reminders, labelled storage and consistent item locations, and practice with a support worker until steps are automatic.

* **Socialisation:** Chloe may find it harder to read social cues, problem-solve during interactions, and manage new or busy environments without guidance. He may prefer familiar settings and people, with reduced confidence initiating or closing conversations or negotiating minor conflicts.

  Supports that may help include structured and predictable community activities, brief scripts and short post-activity reflections.

Overall implications (ABC=82) indicate that without supports, Chloe is at elevated risk of reduced independence across settings. A consistent approach including guided practice, paired with visual prompts will improve accuracy and consistency.

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Chloe is a 24-year-old man with formal diagnoses of Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD) and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), which have been present since childhood. These neurodevelopmental and behavioural conditions significantly impact his executive functioning, emotional regulation, and adaptive capacity across daily life.

Chloe demonstrates reduced functional independence across multiple domains, including planning and organising routines, initiating and completing tasks, and problem-solving in real-time situations. These impairments are further compounded by difficulties with working memory and maintaining attention, as identified in formal assessments (Vineland-3 and BRIEF-A). Chloe requires external prompting, structured routines, and practical supports to complete tasks that others his age typically manage independently.

Although Chloe is physically independent with mobility and personal care, he experiences ongoing back pain which affects his endurance and limits his capacity to engage in physically demanding or prolonged community activities. This has prevented his return to employment and contributes to a sense of frustration and at times social isolation.

Socially, Chloe has limited local connections and reports difficulty initiating friendships and participating in unfamiliar group settings. He relies heavily on his long-term partner and her family for daily support, and although motivated to become more independent, he currently lacks the executive skills and social confidence to do so without structured assistance.

Standardised assessments confirmed moderately low adaptive functioning across all domains, with particular challenges in communication, socialisation, and daily living skills. His BRIEF-A results highlighted significant executive functioning difficulties, especially in working memory, planning/organisation, and task initiation, all of which have a measurable impact on his ability to manage daily routines and engage in employment or training.

Results from the Vineland-3 Domain-Level Interview also indicate that Chloe demonstrates moderately low adaptive functioning across communication, daily living, and socialisation domains. His overall score (ABC=82, 12th percentile) reflects broad functional limitations rather than isolated deficits, aligning with observed challenges in independent living, task completion, and social participation. These findings support the need for structured, consistent supports to build daily functioning skills and independence.

Overall, Chloe requires co-ordinated, capacity-building supports across home, community, and vocational settings to improve participation, develop independent living skills, and achieve greater autonomy in line with his NDIS goals.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support Chloe’s independence, safety, and wellbeing:

**1\. Support Worker**

Chloe currently receives three hours of support worker assistance once per week. This level of support is insufficient to meet his functional and community participation needs, particularly considering his limited routine and lack of social connection. Chloe would benefit from an increase in support worker hours to improve independence, social participation, and capacity for daily living.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Assistance with community access, including attendance at recreational and interest-based groups (e.g., car clubs, social outings), and reducing isolation.  
* Transport support for attending medical appointments, employment services, and grocery shopping.  
* Skill development in meal preparation, shopping, and household cleaning to reduce reliance on informal supports.  
* Assistance to access and engage with employment or training services, particularly where technology use is a barrier.

Increased support worker involvement would facilitate Chloe’s engagement in meaningful activities, improve functional independence, and reduce the long-term reliance on informal supports (e.g., Emily and her family).

**2\. Occupational Therapy**

Chloe would benefit from regular occupational therapy to support his transition toward greater independence and long-term goal achievement. OT input is recommended to address his executive function challenges, build routine, and manage pain-related fatigue.

Key OT Interventions:

* Routine-building support to assist with daily structure, time management, and initiation of tasks.  
* Meal preparation and domestic skill-building, with a focus on safety, independence, and confidence.  
* Fatigue and pain management strategies to improve endurance and functional capacity, especially in preparation for a return to work.  
* Social participation support, including identifying appropriate community activities and confidence-building strategies for group engagement.

Ongoing OT support will promote Chloe’s independence in the home, reduce caregiver burden on informal supports, and enhance his ability to engage in work and community life.

**3\. Physiotherapy**

Chloe reports ongoing back pain that limits his capacity for physical tasks and affects his ability to return to work. He has not yet engaged with allied health services to manage this condition.

Key Focus Areas:

* Assessment and treatment of chronic lower back pain, likely linked to history of clubfoot.  
* Strengthening and core stability exercises to improve physical endurance and reduce injury risk.  
* Education in safe lifting, pacing, and movement techniques to support functional mobility.

Access to physiotherapy will improve Chloe’s physical wellbeing and support his return to employment and active community participation.

**4\. Employment and Training Support**

Chloe is highly motivated to return to work but faces barriers due to chronic pain and executive functioning difficulties, particularly in technology-based training environments.

Key Employment Supports:

* Referral to a Disability Employment Service (DES) or equivalent training provider to support pathway planning and job-readiness.  
* Support completing technology-based or written training modules.  
* On-the-job coaching or mentoring in practical, hands-on roles aligned with his strengths and past work experience.

Employment support would significantly enhance Chloe’s confidence, promote social inclusion, and reduce long-term reliance on funded supports.

**5\. Psychology** 

Chloe has a past history of anxiety and has previously engaged with a psychologist, though he reports no current need for ongoing psychological input. However, given his limited social network and potential for increased stress as he transitions into independent living and employment, access to psychological support should remain available within his plan.

Focus Areas if Needed:

* Managing symptoms of anxiety or low mood.  
* Navigating life transitions, stress, and emotional regulation.  
* Support for confidence-building in social and work-related settings.

Psychological input may prevent escalation of mental health concerns and contribute to Chloe’s long-term wellbeing.

**6\. Support Coordination**

Chloe would benefit from continued access to a support coordinator to ensure effective implementation of his NDIS plan and to navigate available services.

Key Tasks for Coordination:

* Assistance securing independent accommodation.  
* Coordinating allied health referrals and community access.  
* Linking Chloe with employment providers and relevant social/community groups.

Ongoing support coordination will ensure that Chloe remains engaged with appropriate services and progresses toward his stated goals.
