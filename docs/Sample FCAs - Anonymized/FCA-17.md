# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Sarah is a 54-year-old man and participant of the NDIS with a documented primary diagnosis of Schizoaffective Disorder, as recorded in his previous Functional Assessment report . His medical history is complex and marked by significant physical, neurological, and psychological conditions that together impose substantial functional limitations across daily living, mobility, cognition and emotional regulation. Sarah experiences congestive cardiac failure and long-standing Stage 3 Chronic Kidney Disease. His cardiovascular and renal conditions contribute to fluctuating fatigue, reduced endurance and periods of instability that require close medical oversight. He also lives with Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, complicated by peripheral neuropathy causing reduced sensation from the ankles downward, which substantially increases his falls risk and reduces his awareness of foot injuries. Additional comorbidities include obesity, hypertension, anxiety and depression and traits consistent with Cluster B Personality Disorder, each contributing to fluctuations in mood, impulse control and energy levels.

His physical impairments are extensive and include three fused spinal discs, multilevel facet arthritis, hip instability, nerve damage and chronic pain syndromes. These conditions severely restrict his ability to bend, squat or engage in low-level reaching tasks and create episodes of muscle locking, electric-shock sensations and rapid stiffening when he remains still for short periods. 

Sarah also reports long-standing cognitive changes that he attributes to a traumatic brain injury following a motor vehicle accident and describes symptoms consistent with Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE), including slowed processing speed, reduced attention span, impaired memory and fluctuating personality states. His sensory and regulatory difficulties are further compounded by significant light sensitivity, requiring the constant use of sunglasses and heat intolerance that worsens his pain, fatigue and cognitive fog. Sleep disturbance is pronounced, with chronic insomnia, difficulty entering deep sleep states and long-term fatigue that exacerbates emotional and cognitive dysregulation.

Collectively, these conditions interact to create a highly variable and often fragile functional baseline. Sarah’s physical capacity, emotional stability and cognitive clarity fluctuate markedly from day to day, making consistent performance of daily activities extremely challenging and increasing his need for stable, well-coordinated support.

## WHODAS 2.0 {#whodas-2.0}

The adult WHODAS 2.0 is a 36-item standardised measure assessing disability across six functional domains: understanding and communicating, getting around, self-care, getting along with people, life activities and participation in society. It is recognised by the NDIS as a best-practice tool for evaluating functional impairment related to psychosocial and neurodevelopmental disability. The WHODAS provides a percentage of impairment for each domain, with 100% indicating the highest level of disability.

Sarah’s results demonstrate a high level of functional impairment, with an overall disability score of 102/128, placing him in the severe range. His scores reflect substantial limitations across all assessed domains, with the most significant restrictions observed in self-care, life activities and participation in society.

* **Understanding and Communicating:** Sarah scored 13/24 (54%) disability which reflects moderate difficulty in processing information, sustaining attention and communicating effectively during periods of fatigue, pain or emotional overwhelm. He frequently forgets information shortly after it is provided and requires prompts, repetition and structured support to follow conversations and retain new instructions. These difficulties are consistent with his reported cognitive decline, memory impairment and reduced processing speed.  
* **Getting Around:** Sarah scored 15/20 (75%) disability which indicates severe mobility limitations, consistent with Sarah’s fused spinal discs, chronic nerve pain, neuropathy and hip instability. He experiences difficulty walking short distances, is unable to safely navigate uneven surfaces and requires support worker involvement for almost all community mobility. Rapid fatigue, instability and pain significantly reduce his capacity to move around his home and community independently.  
* **Self-Care:** Sarah’s near-maximum score 15/16 (94%) in this domain reflects severe disability in self-care activities. He is unable to safely complete lower-body washing, dressing or foot hygiene due to his inability to bend and reduced sensation in his feet. Showering requires hands-on support and dressing is slow, painful and often incomplete without assistance. His sleep disturbance, fatigue and cognitive impairment further compromise consistency in daily self-care.  
* **Getting Along with People:** Sarah’s score of 15/20 (75%) disability indicates moderate to severe difficulty with interpersonal interaction. Sarah experiences abrupt mood shifts, emotional dysregulation, anxiety and difficulty interpreting social cues, particularly in overstimulating or unpredictable environments. These challenges contribute to strained relationships, social withdrawal and reliance on formal supports for emotional regulation and engagement.  
* **Life Activities:** Sarah’s score of 14/16 (88%) disability reflects severe limitations in completing domestic and household tasks. He is unable to perform cleaning, gardening, laundry or meal preparation independently due to chronic pain, mobility restrictions and reduced endurance. Tasks requiring bending, lifting or prolonged standing are unsafe or unachievable. Without formal support, household hygiene and safety deteriorate rapidly.  
* **Participation in Society:** This extremely high score of 30/32 (94%) disability demonstrates severe restriction in Sarah’s ability to engage in community life. His mobility impairments, anxiety, sensory sensitivities, cognitive fatigue and lack of informal supports significantly limit his capacity to participate in meaningful activities, attend appointments or access community settings without assistance. He relies exclusively on support workers for safe participation in social, recreational, and therapeutic environments.

Sarah’s WHODAS profile reflects a severe level of disability across all domains with a total score of 102/128 (80%) disability. His functional impairments are pervasive, chronic and significantly impact his independence, safety, community participation and capacity to manage daily routines. These findings strongly support the need for ongoing, well-funded support worker hours, multidisciplinary therapy and structured community supports to maintain his wellbeing and prevent further deterioration.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support  independence, safety, and wellbeing:

**1\. Support Worker**

Sarah requires regular support worker assistance to maintain safety, independence and wellbeing. His significant mobility restrictions, chronic pain, cognitive impairments and lack of informal supports mean he is unable to complete personal care, domestic tasks, medication routines or community activities independently. Support workers play a critical role in ensuring his physical safety, preventing self-neglect and facilitating meaningful participation in daily life.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Personal care assistance, including showering below the knee, lower-limb hygiene and safe transfers.  
* Support with meal preparation, laundry, light cleaning, and household organisation to maintain hygiene and reduce physical strain.  
* Assistance with community access for shopping, appointments, hydrotherapy, art therapy and other meaningful activities.  
* Emotional regulation support during periods of distress, pain escalation or cognitive fatigue.  
* Transport support to appointments and essential activities where independent driving is unsafe or fatiguing.

Regular support worker involvement will ensure Sarah remains safe at home, reduce the impact of cognitive impairments on daily functioning and prevent further physical and psychosocial decline.

**2\. Occupational Therapy**

Sarah requires ongoing occupational therapy to address the functional impact of his physical impairments, cognitive decline, fluctuating mental health and limited activity tolerance. OT involvement is necessary to increase independence, reduce falls risk and support the development of sustainable routines.

Recommended Supports:

* Development of structured routines for ADLs, medication management and domestic tasks.  
* Energy conservation strategies, pacing techniques and ergonomic positioning to reduce pain and fatigue.  
* Cognitive strategies to support memory, task initiation, organisation and follow-through.  
* Assessment and trial of assistive equipment for showering, dressing and household tasks.  
* Collaboration with support workers to ensure consistent implementation of functional strategies.

Ongoing OT will improve Sarah’s daily functioning, promote safe engagement in activities and reduce reliance on crisis-based support.

**3\. Physiotherapy**

Physiotherapy is recommended to address Sarah’s chronic pain, muscle stiffness, reduced strength and poor balance. His spinal degeneration, neuropathy and limited mobility place him at high risk of falls and further physical deterioration without targeted intervention.

Recommended Supports:

* A graded strengthening and mobility program tailored to his pain levels and endurance.  
* Balance training and gait retraining to reduce instability, especially on uneven surfaces.  
* Education on safe body mechanics, transfer techniques,and pain management.  
* Monitoring and adjustment of a home exercise program to improve functional capacity.

Physiotherapy will assist Sarah to maintain mobility, improve physical resilience and reduce long-term healthcare risk.

**4\. Hydrotherapy**

Hydrotherapy has been highly beneficial in reducing joint load, improving mobility and supporting pain management. Continued participation will promote cardiovascular health, flexibility and weight management in a low-impact environment.

Recommended Supports:

* Regular hydrotherapy sessions supervised by a trained therapist.  
* Integration of hydro exercises with physiotherapy goals to improve strength and endurance.  
* Use of hydrotherapy as a consistent pain management and mobility maintenance tool.

Hydrotherapy will support Sarah’s ability to move safely, manage pain and improve overall wellbeing.

**5\. Psychology**

Sarah experiences emotional dysregulation, anxiety, trauma-related responses and mood variability that significantly impact daily functioning. He requires ongoing psychological therapy to improve emotional regulation, coping strategies, sleep hygiene and self-management capacity.

Recommended Supports:

* Cognitive-behavioural strategies for anxiety, emotional regulation and stress tolerance.  
* Support for identity loss, social isolation and adjustment to chronic disability.  
* Psychoeducation regarding mood, sleep, arousal regulation and coping mechanisms.  
* Integration of neuropsychological insights to enhance attention, memory and behavioural strategies.

Psychological therapy will improve Sarah’s emotional stability, engagement in daily activitie and resilience during periods of stress.

**6\. Community-Based Engagement Programs**

Sarah benefits from structured, therapeutic activities that support emotional regulation, cognitive stimulation and social connection. Programs such as art therapy, music therapy, tai chi and guided community participation reduce isolation and improve mental health.

Recommended Supports:

* Facilitated access to creative, therapeutic and movement-based programs to enhance wellbeing.  
* Support worker involvement to ensure safe attendance, pacing and emotional regulation in community settings.  
* Regular participation in structured groups that promote routine, motivation and social inclusion.

These programs provide Sarah with meaningful occupation, improved emotional regulation, and opportunities for safe community engagement.

**7\. Support Coordination**

Due to significant cognitive impairments, administrative difficulties and lack of informal supports, Sarah requires ongoing support coordination to ensure his NDIS plan is implemented effectively. His previous plan was critically underfunded due to administrative errors he was unable to identify or correct, highlighting the necessity of ongoing assistance.

Recommended Supports:

* Assistance linking with therapy providers, support workers and community programs.  
* Support with scheduling, budgeting and navigating NDIS systems.  
* Monitoring plan utilisation to ensure supports remain aligned with functional needs.  
* Advocacy to prevent future underfunding or disruptions to essential supports.

Support coordination is essential to reduce the risk of service breakdown and maintain stability in Sarah’s care.
