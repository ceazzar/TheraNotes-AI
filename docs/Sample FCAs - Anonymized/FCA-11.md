# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Marcus has a primary diagnosis of Schizophrenia and Intellectual Disability, both of which significantly impact her cognitive functioning, emotional regulation, insight and ability to manage daily living tasks independently. These conditions affect Marcus’s capacity to plan, sequence, initiate and safely complete everyday activities, as well as her ability to cope with stress, change and reduced support availability.

Marcus’s functional presentation is further complicated by chronic arthritis, which causes persistent joint pain, stiffness, reduced range of movement and functional fatigue affecting both upper and lower limbs. She reports widespread pain across her arms, legs and joints, which significantly limits her capacity for personal care, transfers, domestic tasks and community mobility. Pain is present daily and escalates with activity, resulting in reduced endurance and increased emotional distress.

Marcus also reports a history of Ross River virus, which she believes may contribute to ongoing fatigue, joint pain and reduced physical resilience. While further medical clarification is required, her reported symptoms are consistent with post-viral musculoskeletal and fatigue-related functional impacts.

Current pain management is limited to osteo Panadol and vitamin D supplementation. Marcus reports this regimen provides minimal relief and does not adequately manage her pain during daily activities. As a result, pain frequently interferes with her ability to complete tasks safely and contributes to emotional dysregulation, frustration, sleep disturbance and withdrawal from activity.

The interaction between Marcus’s mental health condition, intellectual disability and chronic physical pain results in a fragile and fluctuating functional baseline. Her capacity varies across the day, with marked deterioration in the late afternoon and evening when fatigue accumulates and formal supports are reduced. Without adequate and consistent assistance, Marcus is at high risk of functional decline, emotional escalation and unsafe situations within the home and community.

## Social Background {#social-background}

Marcus currently lives in Preston with her 29 year old daughter and 2x grandchildren, who provide informal structure, social contact and emotional presence within the home. Her daughter is said to be transitioning into her own accommodation within the coming months, which will result in Marcus spending significantly more time alone, particularly during evenings and overnight periods.

This change in living circumstances will likely have a notable impact on Marcus’s emotional wellbeing and functional stability. She reports increased feelings of loneliness, frustration and distress when alone, particularly after support workers leave for the day. During these periods, Marcus becomes more likely to dwell on pain, unmet needs and perceived stressors from earlier in the day, which contributes to emotional escalation, anger and reduced coping capacity.

Marcus’s ability to self-regulate when distressed is limited by her intellectual disability and schizophrenia and she requires external support, reassurance and structured assistance to de-escalate safely. Extended periods without support place her at increased risk of emotional dysregulation, unsafe decision-making and harm to herself or others. As a result, Marcus’s current social situation highlights a clear need for increased and better-distributed daily supports to maintain safety and stability within her home environment.

Overall, Marcus’s home environment does not adequately support her functional needs. Without appropriate equipment, environmental modifications and ongoing assistance, the home setting significantly increases her risk of injury, pain escalation and functional decline.

## WHODAS 2.0  {#whodas-2.0}

The adult WHODAS 2.0 is a 36-item standardised measure assessing disability across six functional domains: understanding and communicating, getting around, self-care, getting along with people, life activities and  participation in society. It is recognised by the NDIS as a best-practice tool for evaluating functional impairment related to psychosocial and neurodevelopmental disability. The WHODAS provides a percentage of impairment for each domain, with 100% indicating the highest level of disability.

Marcus’s results demonstrate a very high level of functional impairment, with an overall disability score of 110/128, placing her in the severe to extreme range. Her scores reflect substantial limitations across all assessed domains, with the most significant restrictions observed in getting around, participation in society, self-care and life activities.

* **Understanding and Communicating:** Marcus scored 18/24 (75%) disability, which reflects severe difficulty in processing information, sustaining attention, remembering information and communicating effectively. She frequently becomes overwhelmed during conversations, struggles to retain instructions and has difficulty managing phone calls or complex interactions, particularly when fatigued, distressed or in pain. These difficulties are consistent with her intellectual disability and schizophrenia and contribute to frustration, miscommunication and reliance on support workers to assist with understanding information and advocating for her needs.  
* **Getting Around:** Marcus scored 20/20 (100%) disability, indicating extreme mobility impairment. Chronic arthritis-related pain, reduced endurance, poor balance and difficulty with transfers severely restrict her ability to walk, stand and move safely within both the home and community. She is unable to mobilise independently outdoors and remains at high risk of falls, requiring support worker assistance and appropriate equipment for all community mobility.  
* **Self-Care:** Marcus scored 13/16 (81%) disability, reflecting severe impairment in self-care activities. She is unable to independently complete showering, toileting, dressing and grooming due to pain, limited reach, reduced balance and continence issues. Showering and toileting require hands-on assistance to maintain safety, hygiene and dignity. Pain, fatigue and  poor sleep further compromise consistency in daily self-care.  
* **Getting Along with People:** Marcus scored 15/20 (75%) disability, indicating moderate to severe difficulty with interpersonal interaction. She experiences significant challenges with emotional regulation, managing frustration and sustaining conversations. Anxiety, irritability and difficulty interpreting social cues contribute to strained interactions, social withdrawal and reliance on formal supports to assist with emotional regulation and safe engagement with others.  
* **Life Activities:** Marcus scored 13/16 (81%) disability, reflecting severe limitations in domestic and daily life activities. She is unable to manage cleaning, meal preparation, shopping, laundry or home maintenance independently due to chronic pain, mobility restrictions, fatigue and cognitive limitations. Tasks requiring bending, lifting or prolonged standing are unsafe or unachievable. Without formal support, household hygiene, safety and daily routines deteriorate rapidly.  
* **Participation in Society:** Marcus’s near-maximum score of 31/32 (97%) disability demonstrates extreme restriction in community participation. Her mobility impairments, anxiety, emotional dysregulation, pain and cognitive vulnerability significantly limit her capacity to engage in social, recreational and community activities. She is unable to attend community settings independently and relies on support workers to facilitate safe participation and reduce emotional risk, particularly during periods of isolation.

Marcus’s WHODAS profile reflects a severe level of disability across all domains, with a total score of 110/128 (86%) disability. Her functional impairments are chronic and significantly impact her independence, safety, emotional stability and ability to participate meaningfully in daily life. These findings strongly support the need for increased and ongoing support worker hours, allied health intervention, assistive technology and coordinated community supports to maintain Marcus’s wellbeing and prevent further functional and psychosocial deterioration.

## BRIEF-A  {#brief-a}

The BRIEF-A is a standardised clinical measure that assesses an individual’s everyday executive functioning across nine domains, including inhibition, shifting, emotional control, self-monitoring, initiation, working memory, planning, organisation of materials and task monitoring. It evaluates how cognitive and self-regulatory processes impact daily routines, independence, problem-solving and emotional regulation. A higher frequency of “often” responses indicates greater executive functioning impairment.

Marcus’s responses indicate clinically significant executive dysfunction across all domains, with many items endorsed as “often.” Her response pattern demonstrates pervasive difficulties with emotional regulation, initiation, cognitive flexibility, working memory, organisation and task completion, which significantly interfere with her ability to manage daily life independently. These findings are consistent with her schizophrenia, intellectual disability, chronic pain and observed functional presentation.

* **Inhibit:** Marcus endorses frequent difficulty controlling impulses and behavioural responses. She reports making careless mistakes, acting without thinking, saying things impulsively and making decisions that lead to negative consequences. She also endorses frequent angry outbursts and emotional reactions that escalate quickly. These findings indicate marked impairment in behavioural inhibition, contributing to interpersonal conflict, emotional escalation and difficulty maintaining safety and consistency in daily routines.  
* **Shift:** Marcus reports significant difficulty shifting between activities, coping with changes and  adapting to new or unexpected demands. She frequently becomes overwhelmed by large or multi-step tasks and finds it difficult to move from one activity to another. Changes in routine or environment result in increased anxiety, frustration and loss of functional capacity. This profile reflects impaired cognitive flexibility and contributes to avoidance, withdrawal and emotional dysregulation when routines are disrupted.  
* **Emotional Control:** Marcus demonstrates severe impairment in emotional regulation. She frequently endorsed items related to emotional outbursts, becoming upset easily, reacting intensely to minor stressors and difficulty calming once distressed. She experiences rapid escalation of anger, agitation and emotional distress, particularly when in pain, fatigued, or overwhelmed. These responses closely mirror her real life presentation of emotional volatility and difficulty returning to baseline without external support.  
* **Self-Monitor:** Marcus’s responses indicate difficulty recognising the impact of her behaviour on others. She reports not noticing when she has upset others until it is too late, difficulty understanding why people are upset with her and  trouble staying on topic during conversations. These difficulties contribute to interpersonal tension, miscommunication and social withdrawal and  increase her reliance on support workers to assist with communication and emotional regulation.  
* **Initiate:** Marcus reported frequent difficulty initiating tasks independently. She reports needing reminders to begin activities, difficulty getting started even when willing, leaving tasks until the last minute and  spending extended periods inactive. She also reports difficulty generating ideas for how to use her time. These findings reflect severe initiation difficulties, compounded by pain, fatigue, low motivation and  executive dysfunction and  directly impact her ability to engage in personal care, domestic tasks and  meaningful activities without prompting.  
* **Working Memory:** Marcus frequently endorsed difficulties with short-term memory, including forgetting what she is doing mid-task, forgetting instructions shortly after hearing them, losing items and struggling to hold information in mind long enough to complete tasks. These difficulties affect her ability to follow routines, manage personal care, complete household tasks and retain information provided by support workers or health professionals.  
* **Plan/Organise:** Marcus’s responses indicate major impairment in planning, prioritising and organising activities. She reports misjudging how difficult tasks will be, difficulty prioritising activities, failing to plan ahead and struggling to organise daily routines. These deficits significantly limit her capacity to manage domestic responsibilities, appointments and daily structure independently, contributing to reliance on external supports for organisation and planning.  
* **Organisation of Materials:** Marcus endorsed frequent challenges keeping her environment organised, including leaving rooms messy, misplacing important items and difficulty finding belongings. These organisational difficulties increase stress, reduce functional efficiency and  contribute to safety risks and emotional overwhelm within the home environment.  
* **Task Monitoring:** Marcus reports difficulty checking her work for mistakes, rushing through tasks, becoming easily distracted and struggling to complete tasks once started. She fatigues quickly and becomes overwhelmed, leading to incomplete tasks and inconsistent follow-through. These difficulties significantly impact her ability to complete daily living activities independently and safely without supervision or prompting.

Marcus’s BRIEF-A results indicate significant and widespread executive functioning impairment across all assessed domains. She experiences marked difficulties with inhibition, emotional control, task initiation, working memory, organisation, planning, cognitive flexibility and task completion. These challenges manifest as impulsive behaviour, intense emotional reactions, difficulty adapting to change, poor follow-through and reliance on others to structure her day.

These executive functioning difficulties are consistent with Marcus’s schizophrenia, intellectual disability, chronic pain and psychosocial vulnerability and they significantly impair her capacity to manage self-care, household tasks, routines and social interactions independently. Overall, Marcus’s BRIEF-A profile demonstrates a high level of executive dysfunction that substantially limits her independence and strongly supports the need for ongoing therapeutic intervention, structured routines, behaviour support and regular assistance from support workers.

Overall, Marcus’s standardised assessment results confirm that her functional impairments are chronic, severe and unlikely to improve without sustained intervention. Her difficulties are not isolated to a single domain but are pervasive across personal care, domestic functioning, emotional regulation, cognitive organisation, mobility and community participation. The combined WHODAS 2.0 and BRIEF-A findings strongly support the need for high-level, ongoing support, including increased support worker hours, allied health intervention, behaviour support, assistive technology and coordinated service delivery to maintain Marcus’s safety, dignity and  quality of life and to prevent further functional and psychosocial deterioration.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support  independence, safety and wellbeing:

**1\. Support Worker Assistance** 

Marcus requires increased daily support worker assistance to maintain safety, dignity and functional stability across personal care, domestic activities, community access and emotional regulation. Her current allocation of 5 hours per day is insufficient to meet her needs, particularly in the evenings when pain, fatigue and emotional distress escalate.

Due to her schizophrenia, intellectual disability, chronic pain and reduced capacity for independent emotional regulation, Marcus is unable to safely manage extended periods alone. Without adequate support, she is at high risk of emotional escalation, unsafe behaviours, falls and deterioration in mental health and functional capacity.

Recommended use of hours includes:

* Assistance with personal care, including showering, toileting, dressing, grooming and continence management  
* Support with safe transfers, mobility and  falls prevention  
* Assistance with meal routines, light household tasks and daily structure  
* Emotional regulation support, reassurance and de-escalation during periods of distress  
* Evening support to reduce isolation, rumination and behavioural risks   
* Support to access informal and community-based services

An increase to up to 10 hours daily, split between morning and evening shifts, is recommended to reflect Marcus’s functional needs and risk profile.

**2\. Behaviour Support Management** 

Ongoing behaviour support intervention is essential to assist Marcus in managing emotional dysregulation, frustration, anger escalation and anxiety. Her behavioural presentation is closely linked to pain, fatigue, isolation and cognitive limitations and  she requires structured strategies to reduce escalation and improve coping capacity.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Identification of early warning signs of emotional escalation  
* Development of individualised coping and de-escalation strategies  
* Support worker training to ensure consistent responses and approaches  
* Safety planning for periods of increased risk, particularly in the evenings  
* Strategies to reduce distress related to pain, sensory sensitivity and social interactions

Behaviour support is critical to reducing risk to Marcus and others and maintaining her ability to live safely in the community.

**3\. Cleaner** 

Marcus is unable to complete household cleaning tasks independently due to chronic pain, reduced mobility, limited endurance and difficulty bending or lifting. Support worker assistance alone is insufficient to manage heavier cleaning tasks such as bathroom cleaning, floors and rugs.

Without regular professional cleaning, Marcus’s home environment deteriorates rapidly, increasing falls risk, hygiene concerns and emotional distress.

Recommendation:

* Professional cleaning services once per week to maintain hygiene, safety and liveability of the home environment.

**4\. Gardener**

Marcus is unable to manage gardening or outdoor maintenance due to pain, limited mobility and reduced endurance. She is unable to bend, kneel or sustain physical activity required for gardening tasks.

Recommendation:

* Ongoing gardening services to maintain the small backyard area, reduce hazards and support safe access to outdoor spaces.

**5\. Assistive Technology – Electric Mobility Scooter**

Marcus’s community access is significantly restricted by pain, fatigue and  limited walking tolerance. She is unable to mobilise independently beyond short distances and becomes isolated once support workers leave for the day.

An electric mobility scooter would significantly increase Marcus’s independence, allowing her to safely access local amenities (eg. milk bar) without reliance on support workers for every outing. This would reduce isolation, improve mental wellbeing and support dignity and autonomy.

Recommendation:

* Assessment, trial and provision of an electric mobility scooter, including training and safety considerations.

**6\. Assistive Technology – Seating and Furniture**

Low seating throughout Marcus’s home significantly impairs her ability to complete sit-to-stand transfers safely and without excessive pain. These difficulties increase falls risk and exacerbate pain and fatigue.

Recommendations:

* Electric recliner chair to support safe sit-to-stand transfers and allow repositioning to manage pain  
* Height-adjustable dining chair to reduce transfer effort and improve safety during meals and daily activities

These items are essential to reduce injury risk, manage pain and promote functional independence.

**7\. Bed and Bedroom Equipment**

Marcus experiences significant difficulty transferring in and out of bed and repositioning due to pain and stiffness. Poor bed height and lack of support contribute to disrupted sleep, fatigue and reduced daytime functioning. 

Recommendation:

* Provision of an appropriate bed and bed support frame to improve transfers, positioning and  sleep quality.

**8\. Continence Aids and Consumables**

Marcus experiences ongoing urinary and fecal incontinence, which significantly impacts hygiene, dignity and emotional wellbeing. She currently does not have funded continence products.

Recommendation:

* Funding for continence aids and consumables to support hygiene, reduce distress and prevent skin breakdown and infection.

**9\. Support Worker Safety Consumables**

Marcus reports substance use, which presents potential safety risks for both herself and support workers.

Recommendation:

* Funding for support worker safety consumables, including gloves and appropriate needle disposal equipment, to ensure safe service delivery.

**10\. Occupational Therapy**

Marcus requires ongoing occupational therapy to address the functional impact of her physical impairments, cognitive limitations, mental health condition and unsafe home environment. Her chronic pain, reduced mobility, emotional dysregulation and intellectual disability significantly limit her ability to independently complete activities of daily living and manage her environment safely.

Recommended areas of focus include:

* Functional assessment and intervention for personal care tasks including showering, dressing, grooming, toileting and continence management  
* Assessment, prescription and review of assistive technology including bathroom equipment, seating, bed supports and mobility aids  
* Home environment assessment to identify and address environmental barriers contributing to falls risk and pain escalation  
* Development of energy conservation and pacing strategies to reduce fatigue and pain exacerbation  
* Support with establishing structured routines to improve consistency in daily living and emotional regulation  
* Collaboration with support workers to ensure consistent implementation of strategies across daily supports

Ongoing occupational therapy is essential to reduce falls risk, manage pain-related functional decline, improve safety within the home and promote greater independence and dignity in daily activities.

**11\. Physiotherapy**

Physiotherapy is recommended to address Marcus’s chronic arthritis, joint pain, stiffness, reduced strength and limited mobility, all of which significantly impact her functional capacity and increase falls risk. Without targeted physical intervention, Marcus is at risk of further deconditioning, increased pain and reduced independence.

Recommended areas of focus include:

* Development of a graded strengthening and mobility program tailored to Marcus’s pain levels, endurance and functional goals  
* Transfer training (sit-to-stand, bed mobility, car transfers) to improve safety and reduce pain during movement  
* Balance and falls prevention strategies, particularly in relation to fatigue and pain escalation  
* Education in safe body mechanics, pacing and pain management strategies  
* Ongoing review and adjustment of a home exercise program to support maintenance of physical capacity

Physiotherapy will assist Marcus to maintain mobility, improve physical confidence, reduce falls risk and  prevent further functional decline associated with chronic pain and inactivity.

**12\. Support Coordination**

Due to Marcus’s intellectual disability, schizophrenia, cognitive limitations and emotional dysregulation, she is unable to independently navigate the NDIS, coordinate services or advocate for her needs. Without professional support coordination, Marcus is at high risk of service breakdown, underutilisation of supports and disengagement from essential services.

Recommended support coordination activities include:

* Assistance linking and maintaining relationships with support workers, allied health providers and  community services  
* Support with NDIS plan implementation, scheduling and budgeting  
* Ongoing monitoring of plan utilisation to ensure funding aligns with functional needs  
* Advocacy to address service gaps, equipment approvals and housing-related barriers  
* Crisis prevention through early identification of service disruptions and coordination of appropriate responses

Support coordination is essential to maintain continuity of care, prevent deterioration in Marcus’s functional and emotional wellbeing and ensure her NDIS plan is effectively implemented to meet her assessed needs.
