# **Report Overview** {#report-overview}

This report has been prepared based on the information available at the time of assessment, consultation, intervention, and clinical analysis of the client’s goals, circumstances, and needs. While the information is accurate at the time of writing, it should be recognised that many factors can influence a person’s function and performance, and further assessment may be required in the future.

Occupational Therapy Melbourne acknowledges that some aspects of this report may highlight areas of functional difficulty, which may feel confronting to clients and their support. The intention of this report is not to focus on deficits, but rather to identify opportunities for support and recommend strategies and resources that enable the client to live as independently, safely, and meaningfully as possible.

This report is prepared for the named client and relevant stakeholders for the purpose of supporting service delivery, funding, and planning. It contains confidential and privileged information and should be used only for its intended purpose.

# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Ryan is a 14-year-old high school student with a confirmed diagnosis of Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and a co-occurring Anxiety Disorder. Both conditions impact his emotional regulation, executive functioning, and ability to participate consistently in daily life. Autism Spectrum Disorder is characterised by differences in social communication, interaction, and flexibility of thought. For Ryan, this presents as a literal interpretation of language, difficulty understanding nuanced communication, and heightened stress when routines or expectations change suddenly. Despite these challenges, Ryan demonstrates exceptional empathy, a strong sense of fairness, and deep compassion for both people and animals.

Anxiety is a significant barrier to Ryan’s participation at school and in the community. He experiences somatic symptoms such as headaches, stomach tightening, and motion sickness during car travel. These symptoms are often heightened during periods of academic pressure, social conflict, or after sporting events, and can lead to fatigue and reduced engagement. He has a history of psychosocial stressors, including bullying, rejection sensitivity, and self-criticism, which further intensify his anxiety.

Ryan’s medical history also includes asthma, which he manages independently with prescribed medication. He has also been experiencing recurrent headaches for the past seven months, particularly during school weeks, which often resolve with rest over weekends. 

## Social Background

Ryan is described as empathetic, humorous, and socially engaging, particularly in trusted or familiar environments. He has formed a supportive group of friends through school and sport, and is motivated to maintain these relationships. He is very athletic, excelling in running, hurdles, swimming, and basketball, and has competed at high levels in athletics. Despite this, his success has also been a source of bullying by older peers, leading to withdrawal and reduced confidence in social contexts.

Ryan’s social communication is influenced by his autism. He tends to interpret language literally, which can result in misunderstandings, and he sometimes struggles to recognise the underlying reasons for others’ emotions or behaviours. For example, he may believe his mother is upset with him personally when she is frustrated by unfinished chores, rather than understanding the context of her response. These difficulties contribute to heightened self-criticism and low self-esteem. Despite these barriers, Ryan consistently demonstrates strong values, including fairness and advocacy for others, as seen in his efforts to intervene during bullying situations at school.

Ryan aspires to pursue part-time work once he is old enough, particularly in low-stimulation environments such as animal care or lifeguarding. These interests reflect his strengths in empathy, focus, and physical skill. Social participation is meaningful to him, but it can also be exhausting, and he requires periods of downtime after school to recover from the demands of the day.

## Informal Supports {#informal-supports}

Ryan’s mother, Karen, provides his primary informal support. She plays a crucial role in assisting him with organisation, prompting him to complete household responsibilities, and supporting his emotional regulation when overwhelmed. His brother David provides a shared sporting connection, particularly in running, which offers Ryan motivation and companionship. 

Ryan also has newer friendship groups, some of whom live locally and others around 30 minutes away, and these peers are described as supportive and engaged in similar interests. Informal supports remain central to Ryan’s daily functioning, although the reliance on his mother underscores his need for structured interventions to build independence.

## Formal Supports {#formal-supports}

Ryan has trialled various formal supports, though none are currently in place. He previously engaged in speech pathology, which was discontinued as it did not meet his needs, and psychology, which he found beneficial but was unable to attend consistently. He has also accessed occupational therapy in the past, but this ceased due to provider availability. As a younger child, Ryan attended structured camps, but he has since outgrown these programs. 

While he has expressed reluctance to engage a support worker at this stage, future consideration may be warranted as he transitions into adolescence and adulthood, particularly in areas such as driving lessons and preparing for employment.

# **Part B: Mental Health And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-health-and-psychosocial-functioning}

## Emotional Regulation {#emotional-regulation}

Ryan experiences considerable challenges with emotional regulation, which significantly affect his daily functioning. He is prone to mood fluctuations, often becoming flat or withdrawn when feeling overwhelmed, criticised, or unsuccessful. Once he enters a negative emotional state, he finds it difficult to recover without external support and may remain disengaged for extended periods. These difficulties are compounded by his tendency toward rejection sensitivity, whereby feedback or perceived criticism is internalised as personal failure. While Ryan demonstrates a strong drive to achieve and shows motivation to build resilience, he struggles to recognise his strengths and instead focuses disproportionately on perceived shortcomings. His ability to self-regulate declines further during times of heightened stress, such as the end of school terms, when academic and social demands are at their peak.

## Psychosocial Functioning {#psychosocial-functioning}

Despite these challenges, Ryan is empathetic, protective, and values fairness, often advocating for others in situations of perceived injustice. He has supportive friendships and is able to build social connections, particularly through sport. However, social interactions are mentally draining, and Ryan requires periods of solitude to recover from the demands of group environments. School holidays present a mixed experience for him: while he enjoys the freedom from routine, the lack of structure can also lead to increased stress and difficulty initiating meaningful activities. During lockdown periods, Ryan reported improved health and wellbeing, noting reduced illness and less social pressure, which highlights the impact of external demands on his functioning.

Ryan’s anxiety and social communication differences also affect his ability to interpret social cues. He can misread context, taking figurative or colloquial language literally, which sometimes leads to misunderstandings. He may also struggle to discern the reasons behind others’ emotions, often personalising interactions and assuming responsibility when conflicts arise. These difficulties increase his stress in peer settings and contribute to his low self-esteem, despite his considerable talents in academics and sport.

## Behavioural Presentation {#behavioural-presentation}

Ryan presents as cooperative, humorous, and engaging when in supportive environments. He enjoys connecting with others and can demonstrate great warmth and creativity. At the same time, he demonstrates patterns of hyperfocus on particular interests, such as gaming, exercise, and collecting, which can become intense and difficult to moderate. While these focused interests provide enjoyment and a sense of familiarity, they can also lead to imbalances in his daily routine. 

Ryan uses a range of sensory strategies to support his regulation, including noise-cancelling headphones, soft clothing, and, historically, weighted blankets and fidget items. 

Overall, Ryan’s mental health and psychosocial functioning reflect both his vulnerabilities and his strengths. While he experiences significant challenges with anxiety, mood regulation, and social nuance, he is also resilient, empathetic, and motivated to engage in meaningful activities when adequately supported. These characteristics provide a strong foundation for growth, provided he is supported with consistent therapeutic input, structured environments, and strategies to manage overload.

## Child Sensory Profile {#child-sensory-profile}

The Child Sensory Profile 2 (SP2) is a standardised caregiver questionnaire used to evaluate a child’s sensory processing patterns in everyday environments. It measures behavioural responses to sensory input across auditory, visual, tactile, movement, and body position domains. Scores are compared with same-aged peers, with higher percentile ranges indicating greater sensory sensitivity, avoidance, or seeking behaviours. The SP2 assists clinicians in identifying sensory modulation differences that may impact attention, emotional regulation, and participation in daily activities. 

Ryan’s results on the Child Sensory Profile 2 revealed the following:

**Auditory Processing:** More than others (87th–96th percentile)  
Ryan is highly reactive to auditory input, particularly in environments with multiple competing noises, such as classrooms, assemblies, or sporting events. Loud, unpredictable sounds can trigger irritability, tension, or withdrawal, contributing to fatigue and headaches after school days with high noise exposure.

**Visual Processing:** Much more than others (99th percentile)  
Ryan demonstrates heightened visual attention and sensitivity. He becomes overstimulated in bright or visually cluttered environments and prefers low-stimulation settings with softer lighting and minimal movement. This aligns with his reports of headaches around the eyes and fatigue following long school days. Visual overstimulation is one of his strongest sensory triggers and directly contributes to stress and reduced focus.

**Movement Processing:** More than others (86th–96th percentile)  
Ryan experiences increased sensitivity to movement and environmental changes. While he thrives in structured physical activity such as running, basketball, and swimming, unexpected motion causes discomfort and disorientation. Physical exercise acts as a key regulatory outlet for him however, excessive intensity can lead to exhaustion and delayed recovery.

**Touch Processing:** Typical range  
Ryan’s tactile sensitivities are largely managed through established preferences. He avoids restrictive or coarse clothing such as jeans or socks and prefers soft, fitted materials. Physical touch is tolerated in familiar contexts but can be overwhelming in crowded or unpredictable environments. He has developed adaptive coping strategies through clothing choice and routine.

**Body Position and Oral Sensory Processing:** Typical range  
Ryan’s proprioceptive and oral processing scores fall within the typical range. He benefits from proprioceptive “heavy work” through sport, which supports regulation. He does not display significant oral-sensory seeking behaviours, though regular hydration and structured exercise assist in maintaining focus.

**Social-Emotional and Attentional Processing:** Much more than others (97th–99th percentile)  
Ryan experiences very strong emotional and physiological responses to sensory input. When overstimulated, he becomes tense, withdrawn, or visibly distressed. His ability to filter competing sensory information is reduced, resulting in mental fatigue and irritability. He is often unaware of early signs of overload until symptoms (e.g. headaches, stomach tightening) are pronounced.

Overall, Ryan’s sensory profile demonstrates a pattern of heightened sensitivity and sensory avoidance, particularly in visual and auditory domains. These sensitivities directly affect his endurance, concentration, and mood regulation. His ability to remain regulated is dependent on predictable routines, access to quiet recovery spaces, and the consistent use of sensory supports such as headphones, structured exercise, and environmental control.

## Vinelands-3 Domain Level Parent/Caregiver {#vinelands-3-domain-level-parent/caregiver}

The Vineland-3 is a standardised assessment measuring adaptive functioning across Communication, Daily Living Skills, and Socialisation domains. Scores are norm-referenced (Mean \= 100, SD \= 15), with lower scores reflecting greater functional impact. The assessment provides valuable insight into Ryan’s ability to apply practical, social, and conceptual skills in daily life.

Ryan’s results were as follows:

* **Communication:** Standard Score \= 89 (23rd percentile)  
  Ryan demonstrates strong verbal reasoning and expressive language skills. He uses a rich vocabulary and can articulate complex ideas clearly when discussing areas of interest. However, he experiences difficulty interpreting figurative language, sarcasm, and indirect communication. This often leads to social misunderstandings or feelings of rejection. Under stress, Ryan’s speech may become more rigid, and he may struggle to summarise or condense information. He benefits from concise, concrete instructions and reassurance to clarify expectations.  
* **Daily Living Skills:** Standard Score \= 80 (9th percentile)  
  Ryan is independent with personal care, including showering, dressing, and grooming, but requires prompting for household chores and task completion. His executive functioning challenges particularly in initiation, sequencing, and organisation affect his ability to manage responsibilities independently. When fatigued or overloaded, he becomes disengaged from routines such as cleaning and homework organisation. His ability to complete tasks fluctuates in line with stress levels and overall emotional regulation.  
* **Socialisation:** Standard Score \= 79 (8th percentile)  
  Ryan is highly empathetic and motivated to form friendships but experiences difficulty navigating complex social interactions. He struggles to interpret tone, humour, and social nuance, which can result in feelings of confusion or perceived rejection. Although he engages well in structured environments such as sport, he becomes withdrawn or anxious in unstructured social situations. Social exhaustion and the need for downtime after school are consistent patterns reported by his mother.

**Maladaptive Behaviour Scales**  
As part of the Vineland-3, Ryan’s mother completed the Maladaptive Behaviour Scales, which assess emotional and behavioural patterns that interfere with daily functioning.

* **Internalising: Elevated**  
  Ryan exhibits high levels of anxiety, self-criticism, and physiological tension when overwhelmed. He frequently experiences headaches and stomach pain associated with stress and performance pressure. When distressed, he tends to withdraw rather than seek support, consistent with traits of autistic burnout.

* **Externalising: Within Normal Limits**  
  Ryan is typically well-behaved and compliant. He does not present with oppositional or aggressive behaviours. However, during periods of high stress or sensory overload, he may appear irritable, abrupt, or resistant to demands. These responses are short-lived and followed by remorse or fatigue.

The Vineland-3 results demonstrate that Ryan’s adaptive functioning is below his cognitive potential. His strengths in verbal communication and empathy are offset by anxiety, executive functioning challenges, and sensory sensitivities. Emotional regulation and burnout remain key barriers to consistent participation and independence.

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Ryan is a bright, athletic, and insightful young person with Autism Spectrum Disorder and co-occurring anxiety. He demonstrates advanced verbal reasoning, empathy, and motivation to succeed across academic and sporting domains. His strong moral awareness and compassion for others are clear strengths, though these are counterbalanced by ongoing challenges with executive functioning, emotional regulation, and sensory modulation that significantly influence his day-to-day functioning.

Ryan’s sensory profile indicates heightened sensitivity and avoidance, particularly in response to visual and auditory input. He becomes easily overstimulated by noise and visual clutter, leading to headaches, tension, and fatigue by the end of the school week. These findings from the Sensory Profile 2 confirm that he detects and reacts to sensory information more than his peers, particularly in unpredictable or high-demand environments. Ryan manages these challenges with structured exercise, and quiet downtime, though his ability to sustain regulation remains dependent on consistent routines and environmental predictability.

The Vineland-3 Parent/Caregiver Form highlights that Ryan’s adaptive functioning falls below his cognitive potential, with relative strengths in verbal communication and empathy but lower scores in daily living skills and socialisation. He is independent in personal care but requires frequent prompting to initiate and complete household tasks and organisation. His executive functioning difficulties specifically in planning, prioritising, and sustaining focus impact both academic performance and home responsibilities. Socially, Ryan maintains friendships and engages well in structured settings but experiences fatigue and anxiety in unstructured or unpredictable interactions.

Emotionally, Ryan’s anxiety presents as self-criticism, rigidity, and physical symptoms such as stomach tension and headaches. He is sensitive to feedback and prone to internalising stress, often interpreting setbacks as personal failure. These patterns align with the elevated internalising behaviours observed in assessment results and reported by his mother. Externalising behaviours remain within normal limits as he is generally polite, kind, and compliant.

Overall, Ryan’s independence is not limited by a lack of capability but by inconsistent regulation, anxiety, and sensory overload. His performance and engagement are strongest in calm, structured environments that allow for rest and recovery. Without appropriate pacing and support, he is at risk of burnout, reduced participation, and declining confidence. With continued allied health input, school collaboration, and structured routines, Ryan has strong potential to thrive academically, socially, and emotionally. Building his resilience, self-awareness, and executive functioning skills will be essential to supporting his transition through adolescence and toward greater independence.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support  independence, safety, and wellbeing:

**1\. Occupational Therapy**

Ryan requires ongoing occupational therapy to address challenges with executive functioning, sensory processing, and emotional regulation. He demonstrates difficulty sustaining routines, managing overload, and maintaining organisation in his personal space.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Development of structured routines and task management strategies to support schoolwork and household responsibilities.  
* Sensory regulation supports, including personalised strategies to manage noise sensitivity, clothing discomfort, and stress-related stimming behaviours.  
* Executive functioning strategies such as visual planners, checklists, and sequencing aids to improve organisation and task initiation.  
* Collaboration with Ryan’s school to ensure access to a quiet environment, noise reduction strategies, and workload adjustments following absences.

Regular occupational therapy will support Ryan to manage the demands of high school more effectively, reduce anxiety, and build capacity for greater independence at home and in the community.

**2\. Psychology**

Ryan presents with significant anxiety, rejection sensitivity, and low self-esteem, which limit his engagement in both school and social contexts. He also experiences difficulty regulating mood when faced with stress or perceived failure.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Anxiety management strategies, including cognitive-behavioural interventions to reduce physical and emotional symptoms.  
* Emotional regulation and resilience training to build coping strategies for academic pressure and sporting competition.  
* Support to process experiences of bullying and develop positive self-identity.  
* Psychoeducation to improve Ryan’s ability to recognise his strengths and apply self compassion in challenging situations.

Regular psychological input will assist Ryan to manage stress, improve mood regulation, and build confidence across environments.

**3\. Physiotherapy**

Ryan has reported recurrent headaches, which appear linked to stress, posture, and sensory overload. These symptoms affect his concentration and participation at school and in sport. Physiotherapy input is recommended to support his physical wellbeing and optimise his athletic performance.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Musculoskeletal assessment to identify and address postural or tension-related factors contributing to headaches.  
* Development of stretching and strengthening routines tailored to his athletic training program.  
* Education in pacing strategies and recovery to reduce risk of overload during high-intensity training and school periods.

Physiotherapy intervention will assist Ryan to manage somatic symptoms, reduce pain-related barriers, and maintain safe participation in his sporting and academic activities.

**4\. School Supports**

Given the impact of anxiety, sensory sensitivities, and executive functioning challenges on Ryan’s learning, school-based adjustments are strongly recommended.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Access to small group or reduced-stimulation learning environments where possible.  
* Alternative learning formats (e.g., paper-based maths tasks rather than exclusively computer-based) to suit Ryan’s processing style.  
* Adjusted workloads and catch-up plans following absences to reduce stress.  
* Predictable routines, advanced notice of changes, and access to quiet spaces when overwhelmed.  
* Ongoing encouragement to participate in sport while ensuring adequate recovery time to manage fatigue.

These adjustments will allow Ryan to engage more consistently with learning, reduce stress, and maintain participation in both academic and extracurricular activities.

**5\. Support Worker** 

Although Ryan has expressed reluctance to engage a support worker at present, this may become beneficial as he transitions further into adolescence and early adulthood.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Transport support and guidance with driving lessons and community mobility.  
* Assistance in exploring and preparing for part-time employment opportunities, particularly lifeguarding or animal care.  
* Support with community access to manage overload, reduce reliance on family, and build independence.

Engaging a support worker in the future will provide Ryan with structured opportunities to practise independence, develop vocational skills, and gradually reduce his reliance on family for daily functioning.
