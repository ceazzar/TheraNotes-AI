# **Assessment Process** {#assessment-process}

A referral was received for the completion of a functional capacity assessment report to inform NDIS planning and support allocation. The assessment was conducted via telehealth, as per request on Wednesday 2nd, July 2025 with Hannah, who is currently 30 weeks pregnant. Information was gathered through structured interviews and review of informal support. Both the WHODAS 2.0 and CHIEF-SF were administered.

The report is separated into 5 sections:

Part A: About the Participant

Part B: Mental Health and Psychosocial Functioning 

Part C: Functional Impairments Relating to Disability 

Part D: Assessment Findings 

Part E: Summary and Recommendations 

# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Hannah has a formal diagnosis of **Stargardt Disease** and has a past history of **Anxiety**.  

* **Stargardt Disease** is a form of macular degeneration that significantly impacts central vision and is classified as legal blindness. Hannah reports experiencing a “starry” effect in her central visual field, affecting depth perception, object identification, and visual navigation in unfamiliar environments. 

* Hannah also reported having experienced **Anxiety** for a number of years which greatly impacts her ability to live daily life and causes her issues with insomnia. 

## Social Background {#social-background}

Hannah lives in a double-story townhouse, which she has owned since 2020\. Currently, her mother resides with her to provide support during her pregnancy (30 weeks at the time of assessment). Hannah’s mother currently travels between Melbourne and Sydney as she cares for family members up there. Therefore, she is not always helping Hannah although will remain in Melbourne when the baby is first born for a longer period to provide added support. Hannah also has a supportive partner who lives nearby (two suburbs away) and plans to remain involved after the baby’s birth. Hannah’s sister, who lives in Sydney, is also planning to return to support Hannah following the baby’s arrival for a short period of time.

While Hannah has a close network of friends, Hannah reported they provide primarily emotional support rather than practical assistance as many have their own families they are managing. Hannah also noted that it has been difficult to maintain her friendships whilst dealing with her declining vision due to increased feelings of isolation and misunderstanding. Her mother and partner are the primary sources of daily and physical support along with support workers that she accesses up to 3x per week. 

## Formal Supports {#formal-supports}

Hannah currently receives the following supports through NDIS and other community-based services:

* **Support Worker**: 3x per week for 1-2 hours, assisting with shopping, transport, appointments, and meal access.

* **Physiotherapy**: Fortnightly or weekly as needed.

* **Psychology**: Fortnightly; focused on anxiety, sleep, and emotional regulation.

* **Orientation Mobility Instructor**: Provided by Guide Dogs Victoria; annual check-ins and support as needed.

* **Guide Dog**: Hannah has been matched with her guide dog, Digby, for the past three years. While helpful for navigation, Digby has some difficulty with curbs and narrow paths, occasionally putting Hannah at risk of collisions or unsafe situations.

# **Part B: Mental Heart And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-heart-and-psychosocial-functioning}

Hannah has a documented history of anxiety and chronic insomnia, both of which significantly impact her emotional wellbeing, sleep, and functional performance. These conditions are longstanding, with anxiety noted since adolescence and insomnia progressively worsening during early adulthood, particularly after commencing full-time work.

## Anxiety and Emotional Regulation {#anxiety-and-emotional-regulation}

Hannah reports experiencing persistent anxiety that affects her:

* Community mobility \- due to a history of falls and her reliance on a guide dog, she experiences heightened fear when navigating unfamiliar environments. She often requires physical or emotional support to leave the house.

* Social engagement \- Hannah avoids unfamiliar social settings and experiences heightened anxiety in group contexts. Her inability to perceive facial expressions and body language due to central vision loss contributes to discomfort and misinterpretation during interactions.

* Daily planning and task management \- Hannah reports overthinking and difficulty mentally switching off, particularly when she has multiple responsibilities. She has recently experienced increased anxiety due to completing a clinical placement and working multiple days per week, which has led to functional burnout.

Despite these challenges, Hannah demonstrates insight and proactive help-seeking behaviour, engaging regularly with a psychologist on a fortnightly basis. She reports that therapy has been beneficial in developing strategies such as mindfulness, exercise (walking) and structured routine to manage symptoms.

## Sleep and Insomnia {#sleep-and-insomnia}

Hannah experiences chronic sleep disturbance, currently averaging approximately four hours of sleep per night, with frequent nighttime waking and difficulty initiating sleep. She describes her mind as "always active," with racing thoughts and anxiety interfering with rest.

Sleep disruption has a compounding effect on her functional capacity, often reducing her energy, concentration, and ability to engage in daily and social tasks. Of particular concern is Hannah’s historical experience of suicidal ideation when significantly sleep-deprived, which underscores the clinical risk associated with her sleep disorder.

Hannah has trialled both medication and non-pharmacological strategies (e.g., white noise, podcasts, and mindfulness-based activities). While these strategies have offered some relief, sleep remains a primary area of concern that limits her resilience and day-to-day independence.

## Behavioural Presentation {#behavioural-presentation}

Hannah presents as articulate, insightful, and highly motivated. She was open and cooperative during assessments, with good engagement throughout the session. There is no history of aggression, behavioural dysregulation, or cognitive impairment. However, due to her chronic fatigue and anxiety, her functional tolerance is reduced, and she reports regular periods of low energy and overwhelm.

# **Part D: Assessment Findings** {#part-d:-assessment-findings}

Two standardised assessments were administered as part of this Functional Capacity Assessment:

1. World Health Organization Disability Assessment Schedule 2.0 (WHODAS 2.0)  
2. Craig Hospital Inventory of Environmental Factors – Short Form (CHIEF-SF)

## WHODAS 2.0 {#whodas-2.0}

The adult version of the WHODAS 2.0 is a 36-item measure that assesses disability in adults aged 18 years and older. It assesses disability across six domains which include understanding and communicating, getting around, self-care, getting along with people, life activities and participation in society. The WHODAS 2.0 is seen as the first ‘best practice’ assessment for individuals with a primary disability according to the NDIS. 

The outcome of the WHODAS 2.0 provides a percentage of impairment, based on the results of the questions answered, with 100% indicating the highest level of identified disability. 

* **Understanding and Communicating:** Hannah scored 16/30 (53%) disability in this subdomain which identifies the ability for individuals to communicate and understand each other effectively.   
* **Getting Around:** Hannah scored 13/25 (52%) disability in this subdomain, this category looks at someone’s ability to get around their physical environment.   
* **Self-care:** Hannah scored 12/20 (60%) disability in this subdomain, this category looks at someone’s ability to engage in their own self-care.   
* **Getting Along with People:** Hannah scored 13/25 (52%) disability in this subdomain which looks at an individual’s ability to interact with others.   
* **Life Activities:** Hannah scored 27/40 (67%) disability in this subdomain, this category looks at domestic participation.   
* **Participation in Society:** Hannah scored 30/40 (75%) disability in this subdomain, this category looks at an individual’s ability to participate in society. 

The above assessment was completed by Hannah, who scored 111 out of 180 (61%) overall on the WHODAS 2.0, which would rate Hannah as having moderate to severe difficulties with participation in daily activities due to her disability. This assessment indicates that Hannah’s main areas of barrier are her participation in society, life activities and self-care. 

## CHIEF-SF {#chief-sf}

The CHIEF-SF was completed by Hannah as part of this assessment to evaluate the frequency and severity of environmental barriers impacting her ability to engage in everyday activities. This tool explores five domains: Policies, Physical Environment, Attitudes and Support, Services and Assistance, and Technology. Each item is scored based on the frequency of the barrier and the magnitude of the problem it causes.

Hannah’s results indicate moderate to high levels of environmental interference, with the most significant barriers identified in the areas of technology, physical environment, and community attitudes.

* **Technology** emerged as the most disabling environmental barrier. Hannah does not currently use adaptive or assistive technology in the home (e.g., talking timers, safety knives, induction cooktop), which significantly limits her independence and safety in meal preparation. This has led to multiple documented safety incidents, including fire risks and personal injury (burns and cuts). Without accessible technology, Hannah is unable to engage safely in routine domestic tasks, requiring either supervision or support worker assistance. The high scores in this domain reflect not only a lack of access to equipment, but also the disproportionate impact this has on her functional capacity.

* In the **physical environment domain**, Hannah identified structural barriers that restrict her community mobility. She reported frequent challenges with navigating curbs, footpaths, stairs, and public spaces due to the absence of tactile paving, poor lighting contrast, and inconsistent accessibility features. These barriers reduce her confidence in travelling independently, increase falls risk, and contribute to a high level of anxiety when accessing the community. Despite having a guide dog, Hannah continues to experience mobility limitations, as Digby is not always able to detect small obstacles or judge spatial clearance.

* **Attitudes and support from others** were also noted as a source of frequent difficulty. Hannah reported multiple incidents of discrimination, particularly with transport providers such as Uber drivers refusing to allow her guide dog to travel. These interactions are emotionally distressing and reduce Hannah’s access to reliable transport, ultimately increasing her dependence on informal supports. This reflects a broader lack of public understanding of disability rights and contributes to social exclusion and anxiety about community access.

* **Services and assistance** received a moderate score. While Hannah currently receives some NDIS-funded support (a cleaner and a support worker for appointments and shopping), she reported that the current allocation is insufficient, particularly considering her upcoming transition into motherhood. She anticipates increased difficulty managing tasks such as feeding, dressing, and transporting her baby safely without additional support. 

* **Policies** did not emerge as a significant barrier. Hannah did not identify any systemic or legislative restrictions impacting her participation.

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Hannah is a 30-year-old woman with a diagnosis of Stargardt disease, resulting in legal blindness due to macular degeneration. She experiences severe loss of central vision, impacting depth perception, spatial awareness, and her ability to safely engage in visually guided tasks. Hannah’s disability affects her across multiple life domains, including mobility, self-care, domestic participation, and community access. In addition to her vision impairment, she experiences long-standing anxiety and chronic insomnia, both of which significantly reduce her energy levels, functional endurance, and resilience. These factors are compounded by environmental barriers that restrict her capacity to live and participate independently in society.

Hannah demonstrates strong insight, motivation, and a desire to maintain her independence. However, she requires consistent and reliable support to manage her daily needs and safely participate in life activities. This is particularly important as she approaches the birth of her first child, which will place additional demands on her functional and emotional capacity.

Findings from the WHODAS 2.0 indicated a total disability score of 61%, with the most affected domains being Participation in Society (75%), Life Activities (67%), and Self-Care (60%). These results highlight significant limitations in domestic functioning and community engagement.

Findings from the CHIEF-SF identified substantial environmental barriers, particularly in the domains of technology, physical environment, and attitudes/support. These external limitations further restrict Hannah’s ability to manage daily tasks, access the community independently, and maintain safety in her home.

Together, these results provide clear evidence of moderate to severe functional disability, with high support needs across multiple domains, including daily living, therapeutic supports and assistive technology. 

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support Hannah’s independence, safety, and wellbeing:

**1\. Support Worker**  

Hannah currently receives support worker assistance 3x per week for 1–2 hours. This is insufficient to meet her functional needs, particularly given her vision impairment, fatigue, and anticipated transition to motherhood. She requires additional support to safely manage domestic and community tasks.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Assistance with meal preparation, ensuring food safety and minimising injury risk.

* Community access (e.g., shopping, attending medical and baby-related appointments) with guidance and safety support.

* In-home parenting assistance, particularly during early postnatal weeks (e.g., setting up for feeds, navigating with a baby safely, light household support).

* Support with transport, particularly due to recurring barriers with rideshare providers refusing her guide dog.

The assistance of a support worker greatly reduces the risk of injury (burns, falls and cuts). Along with increasing independence and safety in daily activities and improved community engagement and access to healthcare. 

**2\. Occupational Therapy** 

Hannah requires regular OT to address functional barriers stemming from her vision loss, fatigue, anxiety, and transition into parenting.

Key OT Interventions:

* Postnatal function planning: education and task modification for infant care (e.g., nappy changes, safe feeding positioning).

* Fatigue management: pacing, planning, and energy conservation strategies.

* Sleep hygiene support: development of structured evening routines and sleep-promoting environmental changes.

* Home safety assessment: OT to recommend and trial adaptive equipment and assistive technology.

Increased OT support will increase safety, particularly during infant care tasks, improve sleep routines and daily functional tolerance, and enhance independence in domestic roles for Hannah. 

**3\. Assistive Technology** 

Hannah’s safety and independence at home are compromised due to lack of vision-adaptive technology. She has experienced multiple safety incidents due to difficulty with visually guided tasks.

Recommended AT Items:

* Kitchen safety tools: tactile markers, talking timers, level indicators, talking food thermometers, non-slip mats, and safety knives.

* Home accessibility tools: high-contrast labels, voice-activated devices for lighting and alarms.

* Parenting aids (postnatal): front baby carrier suitable for hands-free use; talking thermometer; tactile baby monitor or wearable alert device.

The implementation of this assistive technology would reduce reliance on others for basic tasks, increase safety and confidence during meal preparation and parenting. Along with enabling meaningful engagement in daily life and parenting roles. 

**4\. Orientation Mobility Instructor** 

Hannah experiences regular falls and spatial disorientation in the community. Her current guide dog offers some support but has limitations (difficulty with curbs and spatial judgement). Hannah has expressed concern about her safety once she begins navigating with a baby.

Recommended Services:

* Re-assessment by an orientation mobility instructor.

* Training in navigating with a front carrier or tandem baby/guide dog configuration.

* Environmental navigation planning for local routes to essential locations (clinic, grocery store).

The increased training time with an Orientation Mobility Instructor will improve safety during independent mobility, confidence in accessing the community with an infant and reduce falls and associated anxiety. 

**5\. Psychology** 

Hannah has a history of anxiety and chronic insomnia. These significantly reduce her functional tolerance and increase psychosocial risk, particularly when sleep deprived. 

Recommended Supports:

* Ongoing fortnightly psychological therapy.

* Cognitive behavioural interventions for anxiety and sleep.

* Monitoring and support during the postnatal period (risk of postnatal anxiety/depression due to sleep disruption and stress).

The ongoing support of a psychologist will assist in improving coping strategies and emotional regulation and reduce the functional impact of anxiety. It is also important to remain proactive with the prevention of mental health decline postnatally, meaning Hannah may require increased support during this period.  

**6\. Physiotherapist**

Hannah has a history of frequent falls and reduced physical confidence, which are directly related to her central vision loss and the resulting challenges with spatial awareness and balance. She experiences regular trips and stumbles within the home and community and has previously sustained significant injury. As Hannah prepares for motherhood, the physical demands of lifting, carrying, and caring for an infant will place increased strain on her musculoskeletal system and further elevate her fall risk.

Recommended Supports:

* Ongoing weekly to fortnightly physiotherapy sessions  
* Targeted balance, strength and postnatal training  
* Education and preparation for safe infant handling and movement strategies within the home. 

Engagement in regular physiotherapy will help Hannah develop the physical capacity required to safely manage the functional demands of parenting. It will also assist in reducing the likelihood of falls and injury, promoting confidence in mobility and increasing her independence both at home and in the community.
