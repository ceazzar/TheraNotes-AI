# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Zara has a complex neurodevelopmental and psychosocial disability profile that significantly impacts her functional capacity across daily living, emotional regulation, cognition and community participation. She has confirmed diagnoses of Complex Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (CPTSD) (diagnosed approximately 2017), Autism Spectrum Disorder Level 2 and Sensory Processing Disorder (diagnosed approximately 2020). These conditions are chronic in nature and interact to produce pervasive and ongoing functional impairment. Historical diagnostic documentation is held by Zara’s long-term treating psychologist.

Zara’s CPTSD results in persistent hyperarousal, heightened threat perception, intrusive memories and nightmares, marked avoidance and difficulty regulating emotional and physiological responses to stress. These trauma-related symptoms significantly limit her ability to tolerate unpredictability, engage safely in the community, manage interpersonal demands and sustain daily routines. Periods of increased stress or sensory load commonly precipitate shutdown responses characterised by withdrawal, exhaustion and reduced capacity to initiate even basic tasks.

Autism Spectrum Disorder Level 2 and Sensory Processing Disorder further contribute to Zara’s functional limitations. She experiences pronounced sensory sensitivities, particularly to auditory and visual input, which result in rapid overstimulation, cognitive overload and emotional dysregulation. Zara relies on high levels of masking in order to function in social or professional contexts, which is cognitively and emotionally taxing and leads to significant post-activity fatigue and burnout. Executive functioning differences associated with autism affect task initiation, sequencing, follow-through and the ability to maintain consistent routines without external support.

Zara also has a diagnosis of ADHD, which contributes to difficulties with sustained attention, working memory and task completion. While not the primary NDIS diagnosis, ADHD interacts with her autism and trauma-related symptoms to further impair her ability to manage domestic responsibilities, organise tasks and respond consistently to daily demands.

In addition to her psychosocial and neurodevelopmental diagnoses, Zara experiences chronic physical health issues, including joint pain, migraines, chronic insomnia and an immune-related condition currently under investigation. Chronic sleep disturbance is a significant compounding factor, as ongoing insomnia exacerbates cognitive impairment, emotional dysregulation, sensory sensitivity and fatigue. During periods of heightened stress or reduced capacity, Zara experiences appetite suppression and difficulty maintaining adequate nutrition, with a history of significant weight loss, amenorrhoea and serious medical compromise, indicating the extent to which psychological and physiological stress impact her health and functioning.

The combined impact of CPTSD, autism, sensory processing differences, ADHD and chronic health conditions results in a highly variable and unstable functional profile. While Zara may appear articulate and capable during periods of relative stability, this presentation masks the underlying effort required to function and is not sustainable. On lower-capacity days, which occur regularly, Zara’s ability to complete personal care, household tasks, or engage in the community is severely reduced, with functioning limited to meeting essential needs only. This variability underscores the ongoing and substantial nature of Zara’s disability-related support needs.

This assessment provides valuable insight into Zara’s ability to apply practical, social and conceptual skills in everyday life, particularly where cognitive ability may exceed real-world functional capacity.

* **Communication**

However, their functional communication is significantly impacted by cognitive fatigue, anxiety and executive-functioning difficulties. Zara experiences difficulty initiating communication, responding to messages and managing ongoing correspondence, particularly during periods of stress or reduced capacity. Processing verbal information becomes increasingly difficult when sensory load is high and Zara often requires additional time to organise thoughts and formulate responses. These challenges contribute to reduced participation and interpersonal strain despite intact verbal ability.  
* **Daily Living Skills**  
  Daily Living Skills represent Zara’s most significantly impacted domain. While Zara is capable of completing personal care tasks on higher-capacity days, their ability to initiate, sequence and follow through with daily routines is inconsistent and highly dependent on emotional regulation, sleep quality and sensory load. Executive-functioning difficulties, chronic fatigue and sensory sensitivities substantially reduce Zara’s capacity to manage meal preparation, laundry, cleaning, home organisation and routine household responsibilities independently. During periods of shutdown or burnout, Zara may be unable to complete even basic daily tasks without support. These findings closely align with reported difficulties across ADLs, domestic functioning, safety and routine maintenance.  
* **Socialisation**  
  Although still significantly impacted, socialisation represents a relative strength for Zara. They are empathetic, reflective and value meaningful connection, maintaining close relationships with a small, trusted support network. However, social participation is limited by trauma-related hypervigilance, anxiety and sensory overload. Zara experiences difficulty tolerating unstructured or unpredictable social environments and becomes rapidly fatigued following extended interaction. Periods of withdrawal during burnout further affect their ability to sustain social engagement. Zara participates best in familiar, meaningful contexts but is unable to maintain consistent broader community involvement without support.

Maladaptive Behaviour Scales

## Adult Sensory Profile  {#adult-sensory-profile}

The Adolescent/Adult Sensory Profile is a standardised self-report questionnaire used to evaluate sensory processing patterns in everyday environments. It measures behavioural responses to sensory input across four sensory processing quadrants: Low Registration, Sensation Seeking, Sensory Sensitivity and Sensation Avoiding. Scores are compared with same-aged peers, with higher results indicating greater sensory sensitivity, avoidance or reduced registration of sensory input. This tool assists clinicians in identifying sensory modulation differences that may affect attention, emotional regulation and participation in daily activities.

Zara’s results on the Sensory Profile 2 indicate clinically significant sensory processing differences across multiple quadrants, reflecting a complex sensory profile.

**Low Registration:** More than others  
Zara demonstrates a heightened tendency toward low sensory registration, meaning they may miss or respond slowly to sensory information that others notice automatically. This includes reduced awareness of internal cues such as hunger, fatigue or early signs of emotional overload, as well as difficulty noticing environmental changes unless they become pronounced. This contributes to challenges initiating tasks, maintaining routines and recognising early warning signs of burnout, particularly during periods of stress or exhaustion.

**Sensation Seeking:** Less than others  
Zara does not actively seek additional sensory input and generally avoids novel or stimulating sensory experiences. They prefer predictable, low-stimulus environments and do not rely on sensory-seeking behaviours for regulation. This pattern reflects a nervous system that becomes overwhelmed easily and benefits from controlled, familiar sensory input.

**Sensory Sensitivity:** Much more than others  
Zara is highly reactive to sensory input across multiple modalities, including auditory, visual, tactile and environmental stimuli. Everyday sensory experiences such as background noise, bright or inconsistent lighting, certain textures or unexpected sounds can trigger immediate distress, anxiety, irritability or shutdown. Sensory sensitivity significantly impacts Zara’s ability to tolerate community environments, manage household tasks, maintain emotional regulation and sustain attention throughout the day.

**Sensation Avoiding:** Much more than others  
Zara demonstrates strong sensory avoidance behaviours and actively attempts to limit sensory input in order to remain regulated. They rely on environmental control strategies such as reduced lighting, quiet spaces, predictable routines, selective clothing textures and minimising social exposure. While these strategies support short-term regulation, they also limit community participation and contribute to social withdrawal and isolation when external demands exceed coping capacity.

Overall, Zara’s sensory profile reflects a pattern of heightened sensitivity, active avoidance and reduced registration, meaning they both experience sensory input intensely and become overwhelmed quickly, while also missing early internal or environmental cues. These sensory modulation differences significantly affect Zara’s emotional regulation, endurance, sleep, community access and daily functioning. Their ability to remain regulated is highly dependent on predictable routines, low-sensory environments, access to recovery time and consistent external supports. These findings strongly align with Zara’s reported difficulties across ADLs, domestic tasks, community participation, emotional regulation and burnout vulnerability.

Their home environment can quickly become overwhelming without structured support, which further contributes to emotional distress and functional decline.

Community participation is significantly restricted. Zara experiences severe anxiety associated with leaving the home alone and relies entirely on their neighbour or a support worker to access the community safely. They leave the house approximately once per week, typically only for short, predictable outings with a trusted person. Sensory overload, fear of unpredictable environments and panic symptoms prevent Zara from accessing public transport and limit their engagement in essential tasks such as shopping, attending appointments or participating in social or recreational activities. These restrictions place Zara at ongoing risk of isolation, deterioration in mental health and reduced engagement in meaningful occupations.

Cognitively, Zara demonstrates strengths in visual memory, abstract reasoning and verbal expression however, these strengths coexist with significant impairments in working memory, attention regulation and executive functioning. They rely heavily on list-making and visual systems to compensate for memory gaps, yet still struggle to maintain routines or complete multi-step tasks without external support. Chronic sleep disturbance further impairs cognitive performance, contributing to slower processing, reduced tolerance for stimulation, impaired decision-making and emotional dysregulation. Sleep deprivation also intensifies autistic burnout and trauma-related symptoms, resulting in a further decrease in functional capacity.

Zara’s mental health significantly impacts daily functioning. Hypervigilance, internalised self-blame, chronic self-doubt and emotional exhaustion contribute to avoidance, reduced motivation and challenges with task initiation. Their history of invalidation within medical, social and NDIS systems has eroded trust and increased the emotional load associated with seeking support or accessing services. Without therapeutic intervention, these patterns will continue to reinforce cycles of overexertion followed by collapse, diminishing Zara’s confidence and sense of safety.

Despite these challenges, Zara demonstrates strong insight, motivation for meaningful engagement and a genuine desire to build a life that aligns with their neurodivergent needs and values. They have the cognitive and creative capacity to pursue long-term goals but require consistent scaffolding, emotional support and functional assistance to move toward these aspirations in a sustainable way. Without reinstated psychological, occupational therapy, art therapy and adequate support worker hours, Zara is likely to experience further decline in health, independence and community participation. With appropriate, multidisciplinary supports, Zara has strong potential to stabilise functioning, improve routine consistency, increase independence and build a meaningful, self-directed future.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support independence, safety and wellbeing:

**1\. Occupational Therapy \- Ongoing**

Zara requires ongoing occupational therapy to address functional impacts of CPTSD, ASD, sensory processing differences, ADHD and fluctuating physical health. Previous weekly OT input was highly beneficial but ceased when therapy funding was removed.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Development of structured, sustainable routines for ADLs, IADLs and meaningful occupations, with pacing strategies to reduce overfunctioning/underfunctioning cycles.  
* Executive functioning strategies (visual schedules, task breakdown, external memory aids, prioritisation methods) specifically targeting laundry, cleaning, email/communication and planning.  
* Sensory regulation strategies, including environmental modifications within rental constraints, structured use of sensory tools and planning for community access with reduced overload.  
* Energy conservation and fatigue management, balancing creative work, domestic tasks, social interaction and rest.  
* Support to explore and scaffold alternative work/study pathways that align with Zara’s abilities and health needs.

Regular OT will build Zara’s capacity to manage daily routines more consistently, reduce reliance on informal supports and increase participation in valued activities.

**2\. Psychology \- Reinstatement and Ongoing**

Zara has engaged with their psychologist, Sammy, for many years and sessions are currently greatly reduced due to NDIS withdrawal of funding. Given Zara’s Complex PTSD, anxiety and longstanding trauma, ongoing psychological input is essential, not optional.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Trauma-focused therapy to continue processing cult-related experiences, workplace trauma and internalised negative beliefs.  
* Emotion regulation skills, including recognising early signs of overload, strategies to prevent shutdown and ways to manage flashbacks and nightmares.  
* Support to rebuild self-concept and confidence, challenge shame-based beliefs and develop a more compassionate internal narrative.  
* Assistance with decision-making and self-trust, including graded exposure to making and holding boundaries.

Psychological therapy will reduce symptom severity, improve emotional regulation and support Zara to engage more effectively with OT, support work and community activities.

**3\. Art Therapy \- Weekly**

Art therapy has historically been highly beneficial for Zara as a non-verbal, creative modality for processing trauma and sensory experiences. 

Art therapy is recommended to provide:

* A safe, indirect pathway to process complex emotions and trauma.  
* Support for sensory integration and regulation through creative media.  
* A bridge between psychological insight and embodied emotional expression, especially where talking therapies feel overwhelming or insufficient.

Weekly art therapy sessions will complement psychology and support regulation, self-expression and identity work.

**4\. Psychiatry \- Ongoing Review**

Zara is currently engaged with a psychiatrist (Dr Phillips). Ongoing psychiatric review is recommended to:

* Monitor and optimise medication management for insomnia, anxiety, mood and ADHD.  
* Support careful titration and review of medications to minimise side effects and maximise functional gains.  
* Provide specialist input in complex cases where trauma, neurodivergence and physical health intersect.

**5\. Increased Support Worker Hours (12-15 Hours per Week)**

Zara currently has approximately 5 hours per week of support work, which is insufficient given the level of functional impairment and isolation risk. An increase to 12-15 hours per week is recommended to support.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Community access and participation \- graded outings with a familiar worker to reduce anxiety and increase safe exposure to community environments (eg. cafes, parks, appointments).  
* Assistance with ADLs/IADLs \- support with laundry (including prompt removal of washing), bed linen changes, cleaning tasks and basic organisation to prevent environmental overwhelm.  
* Support for meaningful activities \- attendance at weekly singing/music sessions, time in nature and structured support around writing and creative work.  
* Practical assistance for appointments \- transport support, attending medical and allied health appointments and assistance with follow-up tasks (eg. booking, paperwork).

Increasing support worker involvement will directly reduce isolation, build functional capacity and decrease reliance on informal supports.

**6\. Music Therapy / Structured Singing Sessions \- Weekly**

Zara previously used NDIS funding to attend weekly singing sessions, which provided a vital outlet for self-expression, confidence-building and structured community participation. This has now reduced to once a fortnight at Zara’s own expense.

It is recommended that NDIS fund weekly music therapy/singing sessions as:

* A therapeutic outlet for emotion regulation, breath and body connection and self-confidence.  
* A structured, manageable community activity that motivates Zara to leave the house with support.  
* A way to reduce social isolation and support positive identity.

**7\. Meal Supports \- Continuation and Expansion (Food Street)**

Prepared meals from Food Street have been crucial in maintaining Zara’s nutritional intake when fatigue, low mood or overwhelm are high. Given the history of severe weight loss, sepsis and amenorrhoea, this is not a minor support but a health-protective intervention.

It is recommended that:

* Zara receives funding sufficient for 7 dinner meals per week (ongoing).  
* Consideration be given to occasional additional meals (eg. lunches) during identified high-stress periods.

This will reduce cognitive and physical load, support stable nutritional intake and free capacity for therapy, ADLs and meaningful activities.

**8\. Domestic Cleaning and Gardening Supports**

Current cleaning and gardening supports are insufficient for Zara’s level of fatigue, pain and executive dysfunction.

It is recommended that NDIS fund:

* Cleaning: at least 1.5-2 hours per week, focusing on dusting, surfaces, bathrooms and floors.  
* Gardening: 2 hours per fortnight for lawns plus 3 hours per month for shrub/pruning work.

These supports will maintain a safe, hygienic environment and reduce health risks and overwhelm.

**9\. Respite / Short-Term Accommodation (STA) \- $3,000 Flexibly Applied**

Zara previously had approximately $3,000 allocated for a specific therapeutic respite location in a town associated with their former cult, making it unusable and traumatising in practice. It is recommended that:

* Respite/STA funding of $3,000 be retained or reinstated, but applied flexibly so Zara can access a safe, non-triggering environment away from home when needed.  
* Location choices explicitly exclude regions connected to Zara’s trauma history.

This will allow Zara periods of restorative time away from their current environment, reducing burnout and providing opportunities for intensive support when needed.

**10\. Transport / Travel Allowance \- Retention**

Zara currently has a small but essential travel/transport allowance. Given their limited capacity to drive and complete community travel independently, it is recommended that this transport funding be retained or increased to:

* Support taxis/ride-share to access therapy, medical appointments and community activities.  
* Reduce reliance on informal supports and reduce isolation.

**11\. Dietetics and Nutritional Supplement Support**

Zara has a documented history of appetite suppression during periods of stress, significant weight loss and associated physical health deterioration, including episodes of serious illness. Given Zara’s fluctuating capacity, sensory sensitivities and fatigue, ongoing dietetic support is essential to maintain physical health, energy levels and functional participation.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Individualised nutritional planning that accounts for fluctuating appetite, sensory preferences, fatigue and reduced capacity for meal preparation.  
* Strategies to maintain adequate caloric and nutrient intake during periods of emotional distress or shutdown.  
* Education and support around hydration, pacing and nutrient-dense intake.  
* Access to NDIS-funded nutritional supplements to support adequate intake when Zara is unable to prepare or tolerate solid meals.

Dietetic support and access to supplements will reduce health risks, support stamina and enable Zara to engage more consistently in daily activities and therapeutic supports.

**12\. Personal Training \- Individualised Physical Conditioning**

Zara experiences fluctuating physical health, joint pain, fatigue and reduced stamina, which significantly impact their ability to maintain physical conditioning independently. Individualised personal training is recommended to support safe, graded and consistent physical activity aligned with Zara’s capacity and sensory needs.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Strength building and conditioning to improve physical endurance and joint stability.  
* Graded exercise planning to avoid cycles of overexertion and subsequent shutdown.  
* Injury prevention and body awareness within Zara’s physical limitations.  
* Routine establishment to support consistency and confidence with movement.

It is recommended that Zara receive funding for up to four hours per week of individualised personal training with a trainer of their choice. This support will contribute to improved physical health, confidence and overall functional stability.

**13\. Swimming Pool Membership**

Zara would benefit from access to an annual swimming pool membership to support low-impact, self-paced physical activity. Aquatic exercise provides a joint-protective and sensory-regulating form of movement that is well suited to Zara’s physical and sensory profile.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Low-impact movement to support joint health and pain management.  
* Gentle conditioning on days when higher-intensity exercise is not achievable.  
* Sensory regulation through predictable, calming environments.

Access to a swimming pool will support Zara to maintain physical activity in a flexible and accessible way, promoting consistency rather than avoidance during periods of reduced capacity. This recommendation is supported by previous occupational therapy documentation.

**14\. Pilates and Ballet-Based Strength Building**

Zara would benefit from funded access to Pilates and/or strength-based ballet sessions up to three times per week. These modalities provide structured, predictable movement that supports physical conditioning while also offering sensory and emotional regulation.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Core strength, postural stability and controlled movement.  
* Proprioceptive input to support body awareness and regulation.  
* Gentle, repetitive movement patterns aligned with Zara’s sensory profile.  
* Support for pain management, stamina and emotional wellbeing.

Regular participation in Pilates or ballet-based strength training will support Zara’s physical capacity, reduce pain and fatigue,and contribute to improved daily functioning and participation.
