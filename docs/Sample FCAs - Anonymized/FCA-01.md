## WHODAS 2.0 {#whodas-2.0}

The adult WHODAS 2.0 is a 36-item standardised measure assessing disability across six functional domains: understanding and communicating (cognition), getting around (mobility), self-care, getting along with people, life activities and participation in society. It is recognised by the NDIS as a best-practice tool for evaluating functional impairment related to psychosocial, cognitive and neurodevelopmental disability. Scores reflect the severity of functional impact, with higher scores indicating greater levels of disability.

Aisha’s WHODAS 2.0 self-report results indicate an overall moderate level of functional impairment (Overall Disability: 50/100, Moderate, 94.7th percentile). This profile reflects that Aisha’s disability-related impacts are most prominent in areas requiring consistent routine, sustained task completion, interpersonal functioning and community participation, rather than physical mobility. Aisha also reported that these difficulties were present for 28 of the past 30 days and that she had to cut back or reduce her usual activities for 20 of the past 30 days, highlighting the persistence and day-to-day impact of her functional limitations.

Her scores are as follows:

* **Understanding and Communicating: 45/100 (45%)** This score reflects moderate impairment in Aisha’s cognitive functioning, including concentration, memory, learning and problem-solving. In day-to-day life, this is consistent with difficulties retaining information when overwhelmed, reduced capacity to plan and organise tasks and challenges following through on multi-step demands (eg. appointments, household routines, administration). These difficulties are likely to intensify during periods of psychological distress, poor sleep and fatigue, contributing to inconsistent routine completion and avoidance of cognitively demanding tasks.  
* **Getting Around: 19/100 (19%)** This score indicates mild impairment in mobility and is a relative strength within Aisha’s profile. It suggests Aisha’s primary barriers are not physical movement or transfers, but rather the psychosocial and executive functioning factors that limit her ability to initiate outings, tolerate community demands and access environments consistently during low-capacity periods.  
* **Self-Care: 50/100 (50%)** This score reflects moderate impairment in self-care consistency. While Aisha can complete many personal care tasks independently during higher-capacity periods, her self-care becomes less consistent during depressive episodes or shutdown states, where initiation reduces and routines can deteriorate. This score is consistent with a need for structured routine scaffolding and prompts to maintain consistency, particularly when stress and fatigue escalate.  
* **Getting Along with People: 67/100 (67%)** This score reflects moderate impairment in interpersonal functioning. It indicates significant difficulty managing the demands of interacting with others, particularly under stress, with likely impacts on relationship maintenance, tolerance for conflict and navigating interactions with unfamiliar people. This aligns with Aisha’s reported pattern of withdrawing when overwhelmed and her vulnerability to interpersonal triggers, contributing to isolation risk and reduced help-seeking during low periods.  
* **Life Activities: 60/100 (60%)** This score indicates moderate impairment in domestic and daily life activities, reflecting notable difficulty maintaining household routines and responsibilities consistently. This is consistent with Aisha’s reported challenges with meal preparation, cleaning, laundry and home management when fatigue and depressive symptoms increase and her reliance on supports and prepared meals to maintain basic stability during low-capacity periods. Tasks requiring sustained effort, planning and follow-through are particularly impacted.  
* **Participation in Society: 63/100 (63%)** This score reflects moderate restriction in Aisha’s ability to participate in community life. It suggests significant barriers to engaging in social, recreational and community-based activities in a consistent and sustainable way. This aligns with Aisha’s experience of mental fatigue, sleep disruption, anxiety and withdrawal patterns, which reduce her capacity to leave the house, attend activities and sustain meaningful participation particularly during periods of heightened stress, grief and reduced emotional resilience.

Overall, Aisha’s WHODAS 2.0 profile demonstrates a moderate but persistent level of disability, with greatest functional impacts in getting along with others, life activities and participation. This pattern is consistent with psychosocial disability that significantly disrupts routine maintenance and community engagement and supports the clinical rationale for ongoing, structured supports to reduce isolation risk, improve daily consistency and support functional stability over time.

## BRIEF-A {#brief-a}

The BRIEF-A (Behaviour Rating Inventory of Executive Function \- Adult) is a standardised clinical measure assessing an individual’s everyday executive functioning across nine domains, including inhibition, shifting, emotional control, self-monitoring, initiation, working memory, planning/organisation, organisation of materials and task monitoring. It evaluates how cognitive and self-regulatory processes impact daily routines, independence, problem-solving and emotional regulation. A higher frequency of “often” responses indicates greater executive functioning impairment.

Aisha’s responses indicate significant executive functioning difficulties across multiple domains, with a high number of items endorsed as “often.” Her response pattern reflects pervasive challenges with emotional regulation, initiation, cognitive flexibility, working memory, organisation and task completion, which are consistent with her psychosocial disability profile and observed functional presentation. 

Her domain profile is summarised as follows:

* **Inhibit:** Aisha endorses frequent difficulty with impulse control and behavioural inhibition. She reports patterns consistent with acting or responding quickly under stress including saying things without thinking and difficulty pausing before acting, alongside irritability and anger responses. This profile is consistent with vulnerability to emotional escalation, interpersonal strain and difficulty maintaining consistent routines when distressed.  
* **Shift:** Aisha reports significant difficulty shifting between tasks and coping with changes in routine. She endorses trouble moving from one activity to another, feeling overwhelmed by large tasks and being disturbed by unexpected changes. This reflects reduced cognitive flexibility and contributes to avoidance, shutdown and reduced functional capacity when plans change or demands accumulate.  
* **Emotional Control:** Aisha’s responses indicate prominent impairment in emotional regulation. She frequently endorsed becoming emotionally upset easily, overreacting emotionally, mood changes and emotional outbursts for “little reason.” These difficulties closely align with her reported pattern of distress escalation, reduced tolerance for stress and difficulty returning to baseline without external supports during high-pressure periods.  
* **Self-Monitor:** Aisha reports difficulty noticing the impact of her behaviour on others and recognising social cues in the moment. This can contribute to miscommunication, relationship strain and withdrawal and may increase reliance on trusted supports to assist with reflection, repair and communication during stress.  
* **Initiate:** Aisha endorses frequent difficulty getting started on tasks even when willing, needing reminders to begin and spending extended time inactive and lying around the house. Initiation difficulties directly affect her capacity to commence self-care and domestic tasks, attend appointments reliably and re-engage in meaningful activities during low-capacity periods.  
* **Working Memory:** Aisha endorses difficulties consistent with reduced short-term and working memory, including forgetting what she is doing mid-task, forgetting instructions easily and trouble remembering information even over short intervals. These difficulties impact her ability to follow multi-step routines, retain information from services and complete domestic tasks without prompts, particularly when tired or overwhelmed.  
* **Plan/Organise:** Aisha’s responses indicate major impairment in planning, prioritising and organising tasks. She endorses difficulty prioritising activities, misjudging how difficult tasks will be, not planning ahead and problems organising work/activities. These impairments reduce her ability to manage household responsibilities, appointments and administrative demands independently and consistently.  
* **Organisation of Materials:** Aisha endorses frequent difficulties maintaining an organised environment, including being disorganised, leaving spaces messy, misplacing items and trouble finding belongings. These difficulties increase stress and inefficiency at home, contribute to task avoidance and can compound overwhelm during low periods when capacity to “reset” the environment is reduced.  
* **Task Monitoring:** Aisha reports difficulty checking work for mistakes, rushing through tasks, being easily distracted and having trouble finishing tasks independently. ## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Aisha’s primary areas of functional impairment relate to emotional regulation, executive functioning and routine consistency. During low-capacity periods Aisha experiences functional shutdown, withdrawal from communication and supports, reduced ability to initiate tasks and difficulty sustaining daily routines. This results in inconsistent completion of domestic responsibilities, reduced community participation and increased vulnerability to isolation. Personal care tasks are generally managed independently during periods of stability, however, initiation and consistency reduce when mental health deteriorates, with increased reliance on prompting and structured support to maintain baseline self-care, nutrition and daily structure.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support independence, safety and wellbeing:

**1\. Increased Support Worker Hours**

Ongoing and increased assistance with daily living is recommended to support Aisha to maintain baseline routines and safety, particularly during low-capacity periods characterised by functional shutdown, withdrawal, reduced nutrition and reduced task initiation. While Aisha can complete some tasks independently during higher-capacity periods, her psychosocial disability and executive functioning difficulties significantly reduce her ability to initiate, sequence and follow through with daily tasks consistently without external scaffolding.

Aisha’s WHODAS results indicate greatest impact in life activities, participation and getting along with others and her BRIEF-A response pattern reflects significant impairment in initiation, planning/organisation and task follow-through. Consistent support worker engagement is therefore a key protective factor to reduce isolation risk, maintain routine consistency and prevent escalation.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Routine prompting (getting up, daily structure, hygiene, meals)  
* Meal support (shopping, prompting to eat, simple meal preparation)  
* Domestic tasks (cleaning, laundry, household “reset” during low periods)  
* Supported community access (appointments, errands, meaningful activities)  
* Wellbeing check-ins to reduce withdrawal and isolation

**2\. Occupational Therapy** 

Ongoing occupational therapy is recommended to address Aisha’s functional impairments related to executive functioning, emotional regulation and routine consistency. Aisha experiences significant difficulty maintaining daily structure, completing multi-step domestic tasks and sustaining participation when overwhelmed. These challenges are compounded by grief, sleep disruption and an emotionally triggering home environment and frequently result in task avoidance and functional shutdown.

Aisha’s BRIEF-A profile indicates clinically significant executive functioning difficulty across multiple domains, including initiation, working memory, planning/organisation and task monitoring. OT intervention is required to implement practical systems that remain usable during low-capacity periods and to build sustainable independent living skills over time.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Establishing a “minimum viable routine” for low-capacity days  
* Executive functioning strategies (task breakdown, checklists, visual prompts, cueing systems)  
* Domestic systems (laundry/cleaning cycles, meal routines, simplified organisation)  
* Pacing and graded activation to reduce shutdown and improve follow-through  
* Participation planning to increase consistent engagement in meaningful activity  
* Transition planning for independent housing (establishing routines in a new home)

**3\. Psychology \- Ongoing**

Ongoing psychology is recommended to support Aisha with trauma-informed intervention, emotional regulation, distress tolerance and relapse prevention. Aisha’s psychosocial disability significantly affects her capacity to cope with stressors, maintain routines and remain connected to supports, particularly during periods of grief and heightened interpersonal stress. Continued therapeutic input is critical to support stabilisation and reduce escalation risk.

Psychology support is also likely to assist Aisha to improve sleep-related coping, reduce avoidance patterns and strengthen protective strategies that translate into functional gains in daily living and participation.

**4\. Psychiatry \- Re-engagement**

Psychiatric review is recommended given Aisha’s complex psychosocial profile, longstanding symptoms, sleep disruption and history of escalation requiring hospitalisation. Aisha is not currently linked with psychiatry and specialist input is indicated to support coordinated mental health management alongside her GP and psychologist.

Psychiatric support may assist to strengthen treatment planning during the current high-stress period and provide an additional layer of monitoring and clinical oversight to reduce relapse risk and support functional stability.

**5\. Psychosocial Recovery Coach \- Fortnightly**

Ongoing psychosocial recovery coaching (fortnightly) is recommended to support Aisha to maintain engagement, motivation and goal follow-through during fluctuating capacity. Aisha’s functional presentation includes withdrawal and reduced help-seeking during low periods and she benefits from consistent, structured support to maintain connection with services and progress practical goals.

Recovery coaching is likely to support Aisha to implement routine strategies between appointments, maintain engagement with community participation and reduce periods of shutdown through proactive check-ins and structured planning.

**6\. Support Coordination \- Ongoing**

Ongoing support coordination is recommended due to the complexity of Aisha’s support needs, fluctuating functional capacity and reduced ability to manage service administration consistently. Executive functioning difficulties and stress-related withdrawal reduce her capacity to coordinate providers, collate evidence and respond to planning requirements independently, particularly during low-capacity periods.

Support coordination is required to ensure continuity of supports, effective plan implementation and coordinated service delivery during upcoming transitions, including housing change and ongoing evidence collation for NDIS processes.

**7\. Prepared Meals (eg. Light n Easy)**

Prepared meal supports are recommended to address the significant functional impact Aisha experiences in meal preparation during low-capacity periods. Aisha reports reduced motivation, fatigue and difficulty initiating cooking, leading to reliance on ready-made meals when unwell. Without meal supports, there is an increased risk of inconsistent nutrition, which can exacerbate fatigue, mood instability and functional decline.

Access to prepared meals reduces the functional load on Aisha and supports baseline stability, enabling improved engagement in daily routines, appointments and therapeutic supports.

**8\. Housing Transition Supports**

Targeted housing transition support is recommended as Aisha’s current accommodation is time-limited and emotionally challenging. Housing instability is a significant driver of stress and functional decline for Aisha. Given her executive functioning difficulties, she is likely to experience barriers completing cognitively demanding housing tasks such as inspections, applications, documentation, follow-up, particularly during periods of reduced capacity.

Support is required to assist Aisha to secure stable independent accommodation and to establish sustainable household routines once relocated, reducing ongoing risk of functional shutdown and escalation.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Supported property search, inspections and applications  
* Document preparation, communication and follow-up with agencies/real estates  
* Budgeting and practical planning for moving and set-up  
* Establishing household systems and routines once housed

**9\. Medication Routine Supports (Webster Pack)**

Medication routine scaffolding is recommended as Aisha reports occasional unintentional missed doses when overwhelmed or in a low mental state. This reflects executive functioning and routine breakdown rather than intentional non-adherence. Simplifying medication management through a Webster pack and prompts reduces cognitive load and improves consistency during low-capacity periods.

This support is expected to contribute to improved stability and reduced risk of symptom fluctuation associated with inconsistent routines.

**10\. Consumables**

Funding for relevant consumables is recommended to support Aisha to maintain daily functioning and reduce overwhelm, particularly during low-capacity periods. Given the executive functioning profile identified on the BRIEF-A, practical tools that support organisation, reminders and simplified task completion are likely to improve follow-through and routine consistency.

Consumables should be tailored to Aisha’s needs and may include routine aids, organisational supports, reminder systems and household tools that support hygiene and meal routines.

**11\. Short Term Accommodation (STA) / Respite**

STA/respite is recommended to provide Aisha with planned breaks and recovery periods during times of heightened stress, grief, or when the home environment becomes overwhelming and increases withdrawal risk. Aisha’s capacity reduces significantly with cumulative stress and isolation and proactive respite can function as a protective strategy to reduce escalation and support re-engagement.

Planned STA/respite is expected to support regulation, restore routine and reduce risk of crisis presentations by intervening earlier during escalation periods.

**12\. Supported Community Participation** 

Supported community participation is recommended to reduce isolation risk and support Aisha to re-engage in meaningful activity in a graded and sustainable way. Aisha reports interests including creative activities and reading, however participation is inconsistent due to mental fatigue, low motivation and withdrawal during low periods. WHODAS results indicate moderate restriction in participation and interpersonal functioning, supporting the need for structured support in this area.

Recommended focus areas include:

* Graded participation in meaningful activities (eg. craft/pottery, community activities)  
* Planning and prompting to attend, with debriefing and regulation strategies  
* Building consistent weekly structure to reduce isolation and support wellbeing
