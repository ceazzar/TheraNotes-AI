# **Report Overview** {#report-overview}

This report has been prepared based on the information available at the time of assessment, consultation, intervention, and clinical analysis of the client’s goals, circumstances, and needs. While the information is accurate at the time of writing, it should be recognised that many factors can influence a person’s function and performance, and further assessment may be required in the future.

Occupational Therapy Melbourne acknowledges that some aspects of this report may highlight areas of functional difficulty, which may feel confronting to clients and their support. The intention of this report is not to focus on deficits, but rather to identify opportunities for support and recommend strategies and resources that enable the client to live as independently, safely, and meaningfully as possible.

This report is prepared for the named client and relevant stakeholders for the purpose of supporting service delivery, funding, and planning. It contains confidential and privileged information and should be used only for its intended purpose.

# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Thomas has a confirmed diagnosis of Autism Spectrum Disorder, identified at 3.5 years of age after developmental differences were first noted from approximately six months. From an early age, he demonstrated an advanced vocabulary and ability to hold detailed conversations, often using scripted or unusual language. He has always shown a strong attention to detail and intense interests, with early obsessions including Thomas the Tank Engine trains and geography. His developmental history has also been marked by proprioceptive seeking behaviours, such as frequent climbing and falling, and reduced safety awareness.

Thomas engaged in extensive occupational therapy and speech pathology during his early years, which supported fine motor development, social skills, and flexible thinking. Despite these interventions, he experienced significant meltdowns during his first years at school, lasting two to three hours at times. These meltdowns have reduced in frequency and now occur approximately once or twice per year, typically triggered by autistic burnout. Thomas continues to use showers and exercise as core self-regulation strategies. He is highly intelligent, with an IQ assessed at around 130, and has achieved strongly in both academic and sporting domains. However, his perfectionism and drive to outperform others contribute to stress, anxiety, and physical exhaustion. His limited interoceptive awareness (difficulty recognising illness, pain, and fatigue) further increases the risk of burnout.

## Social Background {#social-background}

Initially, Thomas demonstrated little interest in peers and required scripted prompts to engage socially. Over time, his interactions have become more natural, and he has maintained long-standing friendships from primary school through to high school. He transitioned to a smaller high school setting, which better supports his need for predictability and reduced social demands. Thomas continues to mask his challenges in community and school settings, often presenting as capable and confident, but experiences exhaustion and emotional release at home. He requires solitude to recover after busy social days and is at risk of emotional overwhelm if overstimulated. Despite these challenges, Thomas demonstrates empathy, a strong sense of justice, and a capacity for meaningful relationships.

## Informal Supports

Thomas’s primary source of daily support is his mother, Karen, who provides structure, prompting, and emotional support. She plays a central role in assisting Thomas to manage his routines, regulate his emotions, and participate in the community. Thomas has a younger brother, with whom he shares a bedroom. 

## Formal Supports

Formal supports have included significant occupational therapy and speech pathology during early childhood, which were gradually reduced as he demonstrated improved skills. Thomas continues to access a school psychologist, although sessions often involve informal engagement rather than targeted therapy. He has also accessed occupational therapy assessments more recently to address sensory regulation needs.

## Home Environment

Thomas lives in the family home with his mother and two brothers, with Thomas being the middle child. He shares a bedroom with his younger brother, which contributes to occasional tension and disruption of his routines. Thomas is generally organised and prefers clean, structured spaces, though his ability to maintain this standard varies, particularly during stressful periods such as the end of the school term. He demonstrates hyperfocus on detail, often spending prolonged periods organising minor tasks (such as a spice drawer) while neglecting larger household responsibilities. Thomas contributes to domestic activities such as meal preparation, dishes, and caring for animals but requires prompting to follow through consistently. His home environment remains a safe and supportive base where he can remove the mask he wears in public, recover from social exhaustion, and regulate his emotions.

# **Part B: Mental Health And Psychosocial Functioning** {#part-b:-mental-health-and-psychosocial-functioning}

## Emotional Regulation

Thomas’s emotional regulation has improved considerably since early childhood but remains a core area of vulnerability. As a younger child, he experienced frequent and severe meltdowns, often lasting up to two to three hours. These were triggered by sensory overload, unexpected changes, or perceived loss of control. While their frequency has significantly reduced, Thomas continues to experience meltdowns approximately once or twice per year, usually in the context of autistic burnout when prolonged stress and fatigue accumulate.

On a daily basis, Thomas demonstrates heightened sensitivity to stress and can quickly become tense, irritable, or reactive when overwhelmed. His anxiety is particularly evident in the lead-up to sporting competitions, where he places immense pressure on himself to succeed. This often manifests as disorganisation, irritability, or appearing rude, though it stems from internal stress. Thomas tends to deny or minimise his anxiety, despite clear physiological signs such as headaches, stomach pain, and tearfulness. Over time, he has developed adaptive strategies, including the use of exercise and showers to regulate his emotional state, and he benefits from proactive preparation the night before important events. However, his reliance on these strategies highlights the ongoing need for structured supports to build flexibility and resilience in emotional regulation.

## Psychosocial Functioning

Thomas is socially capable and able to form and maintain meaningful friendships, but this capacity comes at a cost. He has long-standing friends from primary school and has integrated into a smaller high school environment with relative ease. He has also maintained a long-term romantic relationship, demonstrating his ability to build close, reciprocal bonds. Despite this, Thomas continues to experience significant social fatigue and requires extended periods of solitude to recover after busy or emotionally demanding days.

Masking remains a prominent feature of Thomas’s social functioning. At school and in community settings he presents as confident, capable, and socially appropriate, which has reduced his access to supports as teachers and peers often do not see the extent of his challenges. At home, the effects of masking become apparent, with emotional outbursts, withdrawal, and physical complaints such as headaches and stomach pain. Thomas’s social motivation is therefore balanced by the risks of overexertion, highlighting the importance of recognising his need for downtime and recovery.

Thomas demonstrates empathy and a strong sense of justice, often stepping in when he perceives others are being treated unfairly. These traits reflect maturity and moral awareness, but they also increase his stress when he feels powerless to change a situation. 

## Behavioural Presentation

In the home environment Thomas can become reactive, tense, and oppositional when demands exceed his coping capacity. His body language reflects this, with visible tension and rigidity. However, in the community he masks these behaviours and rarely displays externalised dysregulation. Instead, his difficulties often emerge as withdrawal, shutdown, or delayed emotional release once in a safe environment. Thomas is cooperative and responsive when provided with clear strategies and structure, and he demonstrates insight into his triggers, implementing known coping mechanisms when prompted.

## Mental Health Considerations

Thomas’s profile reflects a combination of protective and risk factors. Protective factors include his high intelligence, strong motivation to achieve, ability to form close and lasting relationships, supportive family environment, and use of exercise and animals as regulating outlets. These strengths provide resilience and the capacity to learn and apply strategies when supported. Risk factors include his perfectionism, tendency to mask difficulties, denial of anxiety, sensory sensitivities, and very limited interoceptive awareness. These vulnerabilities place him at ongoing risk of autistic burnout, heightened stress, and emotional exhaustion if demands are not carefully balanced with recovery opportunities.

# **Part D: Assessment Findings** {#part-d:-assessment-findings}

## Child Sensory Profile 2

The Child Sensory Profile 2 (SP2) is a standardised caregiver questionnaire used to evaluate a child’s sensory processing patterns in everyday environments. It assesses behavioural responses to sensory input across the domains of auditory, visual, tactile, movement, and body position processing. Scores are interpreted relative to same-aged peers, with higher percentile ranges indicating increased sensory sensitivity or seeking behaviours. The SP2 assists clinicians in identifying sensory modulation patterns that may affect attention, emotional regulation, and participation in daily activities.

Thomas’s results on the Child Sensory Profile 2 revealed the following:

**Auditory Processing:** More than others (86th–96th percentile)  
Thomas is highly reactive to auditory stimuli, particularly loud or unexpected noises such as sirens. He often covers his ears to reduce discomfort and experiences difficulty concentrating when background noise is present. This aligns with reports of increased fatigue and irritability following noisy school days.

**Visual Processing:** Much more than others (99th percentile)  
Thomas demonstrates heightened visual attention and sensitivity. He becomes overstimulated in bright or visually busy environments and performs best in structured, low-clutter spaces. Visual overstimulation contributes to headaches, fatigue, and difficulty sustaining focus.

**Touch Processing:** Much more than others (97th–99th percentile)  
Thomas is extremely sensitive to tactile input and avoids certain clothing textures and tight-fitting garments. He prefers soft, fleecy fabrics. Unexpected touch can trigger irritability or withdrawal, though deep pressure activities (e.g. leaning on furniture, using a fit ball) are calming.

**Movement Processing:** More than others (86th–96th percentile)  
Thomas seeks out strong movement experiences and benefits from physical activity such as running and ninja-warrior training for regulation. However, proprioceptive awareness remains inconsistent, he may appear clumsy, bump into objects, or underestimate physical risks. His very high pain threshold contributes to delayed recognition of injury or fatigue.

**Oral Sensory Processing:** Typical range  
Thomas engages in mild oral-sensory seeking behaviours, including chewing gum and frequent water drinking. These habits help manage attention and anxiety during the school day. His eating range is functional but influenced by mild textural preferences.

Thomas’s overall sensory profile demonstrates a mixed pattern of hypersensitivity and sensory seeking. He detects more sensory information than peers yet inconsistently registers internal cues such as hunger, fatigue, or pain. When overstimulated, Thomas becomes tense, reactive, or withdrawn, requiring time and sensory regulation strategies to recover. These findings explain his perfectionistic tendencies, need for structure, and susceptibility to burnout when demands exceed coping capacity.

The SP2 results confirm that Thomas experiences significant sensory modulation differences across auditory, tactile, and proprioceptive domains. His ability to maintain regulation is highly dependent on predictable environments and consistent use of sensory supports.

## Vinelands-3 Domain Level Parent/Caregiver

The Vineland-3 Domain Level Parent/Caregiver Form is a standardised assessment that measures a child’s adaptive functioning across communication, daily living and socialisation domains. Results are norm-referenced (Mean \= 100, SD \= 15), with lower scores indicating greater functional impact. 

Thomas's results were as follows:

* **Communication:** Standard Score \= 86 (18th percentile)  
  Thomas’s communication skills are above average in structured or familiar contexts. He uses advanced vocabulary and complex sentence structures but has difficulty summarising or filtering detail. Under stress or fatigue, he may revert to overly detailed or scripted speech patterns. He benefits from concise, step-by-step instructions and extra processing time when given complex directions.  
* **Daily Living Skills:** Standard Score \= 78 (7th percentile)  
  Thomas is independent in self-care routines such as showering, dressing, and grooming but requires regular prompting to initiate and complete household tasks. Executive functioning challenges affect his ability to plan, organise, and follow through. He often hyper-focuses on small details while neglecting broader priorities therefore, reducing efficiency.  
* **Socialisation:** Standard Score \= 81 (10th percentile)  
  Thomas has strong social motivation and empathy and has maintained close friendships since primary school. However, he experiences social fatigue after extended interaction and requires time alone to recover. Masking behaviours at school contribute to exhaustion and delayed emotional processing, consistent with autistic burnout.

**Maladaptive Behaviour Scales**

As part of the Vineland-3, Thomas's mother also completed the Maladaptive Behaviour Scales, which assess emotional and behavioural concerns that may interfere with adaptive functioning.

* **Internalising:** Elevated.   
  Thomas displays heightened internalising behaviours including anxiety, worry, and self-critical thinking. He experiences physiological symptoms such as stomach aches and headaches linked to stress and perfectionism. When overwhelmed, he withdraws or becomes non-verbal rather than seeking help. These tendencies align with episodes of autistic burnout and the masking-related fatigue observed by his mother.  
* **Externalising:** Within normal limits.  
  Thomas is generally compliant and well-behaved. He rarely demonstrates outward aggression or defiance; however, under extreme stress or sensory overload, he can appear abrupt or oppositional. Such behaviours are short-lived and typically followed by remorse once he is calm.

The Vineland-3 results show adaptive functioning below Thomas’s cognitive potential. His strengths in language, learning, and physical ability are offset by sensory sensitivities, rigid routines, and executive-functioning difficulties. Emotional regulation and burnout remain primary barriers to consistent participation and independence.

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Thomas is a bright, articulate, and high-achieving young person with Autism Spectrum Disorder whose strong cognitive and verbal skills mask the significant impact of his sensory, emotional, and executive-functioning differences. Although independent with basic self-care and highly capable in both school and sport, Thomas’s sensory sensitivities, anxiety, and perfectionism substantially limit his consistency and independence. He functions best in predictable, structured environments and becomes easily overwhelmed when demands increase or unexpected change occurs.

The standardised assessments support these observations. The Child Sensory Profile 2 identifies heightened sensitivity to auditory, tactile, and visual input, alongside sensory-seeking tendencies that Thomas uses to self-regulate. These patterns explain his reliance on movement, showers, and physical exercise to manage emotional overload. The Vineland-3 results show adaptive functioning below Thomas’s cognitive potential, highlighting relative strengths in communication and social awareness but reduced independence in daily living and organisation. Together, these findings demonstrate that Thomas’s challenges stem not from a lack of skill but from difficulty sustaining regulation and flexibility when under pressure.

Functionally, Thomas presents as detail-oriented and driven, yet struggles to prioritise, pace himself, and interpret internal cues such as fatigue or hunger. His perfectionistic thinking often results in over-effort and emotional exhaustion, while sensory overload and masking contribute to anxiety and burnout. He is cooperative and motivated but relies heavily on his mother’s support to manage routines, transitions, and competing expectations.

To support Thomas’s ongoing development and wellbeing, a multidisciplinary approach is recommended. Occupational therapy should focus on improving sensory regulation, executive functioning, and interoceptive awareness through structured yet flexible routines, visual planning tools, and proactive coping strategies. Psychological support is needed to address anxiety, perfectionism, and self-acceptance, helping Thomas to recognise limits and recover before reaching burnout. Support-worker involvement is also advised to reinforce these strategies in daily life, providing practical assistance with organisation, community participation, and domestic tasks while reducing caregiver burden.

With consistent allied health input and structured supports, Thomas can continue to thrive academically and athletically while developing the insight and regulation skills necessary for balanced, independent living. These interventions aim to reduce the impact of sensory and emotional overload, strengthen Thomas’s self-management, and promote sustained participation across home, school, and community environments.

## Recommendations {#recommendations}

Based on the clinical assessment and standardised tools, the following recommendations are made to support independence, safety, and wellbeing:

### **1\. Occupational Therapy**

Thomas requires ongoing occupational therapy to address challenges with sensory regulation, executive functioning, and interoceptive awareness. While he demonstrates independence in self-care, he continues to experience significant difficulties managing organisation, recognising fatigue or illness, and sustaining routines across school, sport, and home life.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Development of structured routines and task management strategies to support balance between schoolwork, sport, and household responsibilities.  
* Sensory regulation supports, including personalised strategies to reduce reliance on reactive coping (e.g. showering, gum) and expand proactive calming options.  
* Executive functioning strategies such as visual planners, sequencing aids, and checklists to improve flexibility, organisation, and task follow-through.  
* Interoception training to build awareness of fatigue, pain, and illness to reduce health and safety risks.  
* Collaboration with Thomas’s school and sporting programs to ensure realistic workload expectations, advanced notice of changes, and access to recovery strategies.

Regular occupational therapy will support Thomas to manage high-performance environments more sustainably, reduce anxiety, and maintain functional independence across settings.

**2\. Psychology**

Thomas presents with perfectionism, denial of anxiety, and emotional dysregulation under stress, which impact his participation in school, sport, and social settings. His tendency to mask difficulties in the community also reduces access to natural supports. Ongoing psychological input is recommended.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Anxiety management strategies, including cognitive-behavioural interventions and relaxation techniques to reduce physical and emotional symptoms.  
* Emotional regulation and resilience training to support Thomas in managing the pressure of academics and competitive sport.  
* Cognitive reframing to address perfectionistic tendencies and develop a more flexible, self-compassionate mindset.  
* Psychoeducation to support Thomas in recognising and accepting his autistic identity, reducing the drive to mask and preventing autistic burnout.

Psychological intervention will assist Thomas to manage stress more effectively, improve mood regulation, and build confidence in both academic and social environments.

**3\. School Supports**

Given the impact of Thomas’s anxiety, sensory sensitivities, and executive-functioning challenges on his learning, school-based adjustments are recommended to support his regulation and participation.

Recommended Areas of Focus:

* Access to quiet or reduced-stimulation environments during periods of overload or high cognitive demand.  
* Use of alternative learning formats (e.g., written or paper-based tasks) when noise, screens, or visual complexity increase stress.  
* Adjusted workloads and structured catch-up plans following absences or periods of fatigue.  
* Predictable routines and advance notice of changes to minimise anxiety.  
* Access to quiet spaces for breaks, especially after socially or sensorily demanding activities.  
* Encouragement to participate in sport with appropriate pacing and recovery time.

These supports will help Thomas maintain consistent engagement with learning, reduce stress, and support participation across both academic and extracurricular activities.

**4\. Support Worker**

Although Thomas is independent in many personal tasks, he relies heavily on his mother for organisation, prompting, and managing competing demands. Support worker involvement is recommended to reduce caregiver burden and build Thomas’s independence.

Recommended Use of Hours:

* Assistance with organisation, including use of visual schedules, planning tools, and reminders to complete domestic and school-related responsibilities.  
* Support with community access, particularly around sporting competitions and social outings, to reduce anxiety, ensure preparation, and promote independence.  
* Reinforcement of skills in cooking, cleaning, laundry, and shopping in structured, capacity-building contexts.  
* Guidance in recognising and respecting limits, supporting Thomas to balance high academic and sporting expectations with adequate downtime.

Regular support worker input will promote Thomas’s independence, improve social participation, and safeguard against long-term reliance on family supports.
