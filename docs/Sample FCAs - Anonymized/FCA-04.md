# **Report Overview** {#report-overview}

This report has been prepared based on the information available at the time of assessment, consultation, intervention, and clinical analysis of the client’s goals, circumstances, and needs. While the information is accurate at the time of writing, it should be recognised that many factors can influence a person’s function and performance, and further assessment may be required in the future.

Occupational Therapy Melbourne acknowledges that some aspects of this report may highlight areas of functional difficulty, which may feel confronting to clients and their support. The intention of this report is not to focus on deficits, but rather to identify the impact of the client’s permanent physical condition on their functional abilities and capacity to engage in employment. 

Specifically, this report has been prepared to assist in determining the client’s eligibility to access their superannuation on the grounds of permanent incapacity, where a medical condition is likely to prevent a return to work for which the individual is reasonably qualified by education, training or experience.

This report is prepared for the named client and relevant stakeholders for the purpose of work capacity, safety considerations and long-term prognosis. It contains confidential and privileged information and should be used only for its intended purpose.

# **Part A: About The Participant** {#part-a:-about-the-participant}

## Primary Diagnosis and Medical History  {#primary-diagnosis-and-medical-history }

Emma has a significant and well-documented history of cervical spinal trauma with associated neurological impairment. In 2018, he sustained a traumatic injury resulting in a C5-C6 disc extrusion with spinal cord compression and nerve root involvement, leading to cervical myelopathy and radiculopathy. Surgical intervention was undertaken in the form of a C5-C6 anterior cervical discectomy and fusion.

Despite surgery, Emma has continued to experience persistent neurological symptoms and chronic pain, indicating incomplete neurological recovery. Serial MRI imaging over subsequent years confirms ongoing spinal cord signal changes consistent with myelomalacia, multilevel neural foraminal stenosis and compression of multiple cervical nerve roots. These findings are consistent with permanent neurological involvement rather than an isolated or resolved injury.

Functionally, Emma reports chronic neck pain with associated upper and lower limb symptoms including pins and needles, numbness, altered sensation and reduced motor control. Pain levels fluctuate daily, with baseline pain reported at approximately 4/10 on low-activity days, increasing to 8/10 with physical exertion, particularly during lifting, sustained postures, repetitive upper limb use or prolonged activity. These symptoms significantly limit Emma’s physical endurance and tolerance for sustained tasks.

Emma has also experienced ongoing bowel dysfunction since the injury, attributed to nerve damage, which has remained unchanged despite the passage of time and surgical intervention. This further supports the presence of permanent neurological damage. 

Emma continued to work for several years following surgery despite increasing symptoms, indicating a high level of motivation and attempts to compensate for functional limitations. However, his capacity to sustain work demands progressively declined. Approximately six months prior to this assessment, Emma ceased working due to escalating pain, fatigue and reduced functional tolerance. He has since sold his business, reflecting the extent to which his condition has impacted his vocational capacity.

## Social Background {#social-background}

Emma lives with his partner and two young children aged 3 and 5 years. He remains emotionally invested in his role as a parent and partner however, his physical limitations significantly affect his ability to engage in many aspects of family life. In particular, Emma reports difficulty with lifting, carrying and physically assisting his children and he frequently needs to explain to them why he cannot participate in certain activities.

While Emma describes his partner as managing household responsibilities “reasonably well,” this is achieved through his partner assuming a greater share of physically demanding tasks. Emma’s reduced physical capacity has resulted in an altered distribution of responsibilities within the household, with increased reliance on his partner to maintain daily functioning and family routines.

Emma’s social engagement outside the family is limited by pain, fatigue and reduced tolerance for prolonged activity. Activities that were previously meaningful to him, including sport and physically demanding leisure pursuits, have been significantly reduced or ceased altogether due to symptom exacerbation.

## Formal Supports {#formal-supports}

Emma is not currently engaged in regular allied health or specialist services. Following his cervical fusion surgery, he participated in a period of physiotherapy however, this was not continued long term. At present, Emma manages his condition primarily through activity modification and pacing.

Emma attends his general practitioner intermittently, typically during periods of increased pain or functional decline. 

## Informal Supports  {#informal-supports}

Emma’s partner is his primary informal support and provides assistance across a range of daily activities, including meal preparation, cleaning, shopping, household management and physically demanding parenting tasks. While family and friends are available to assist on an occasional basis if required, Emma’s day-to-day functioning is heavily reliant on his partner’s ongoing support.

This reliance reflects not a lack of motivation or skill, but rather the physical limitations imposed by Emma’s condition and the need to avoid activities that exacerbate pain or neurological symptoms.

## Home Environment  {#home-environment}

Emma resides in a standard residential property with relatively flat terrain and no significant environmental barriers identified within the home. The physical layout of the home itself does not present access issues however, the functional demands associated with daily living in the home, including caring for young children, managing domestic tasks and maintaining the property and surrounding land, exceed Emma’s current physical capacity.

Tasks such as gardening, property maintenance and prolonged outdoor activities require significant pacing, task breakdown and extended recovery time and frequently result in increased pain and fatigue. As a result, Emma must limit his participation in these activities or rely on assistance to prevent symptom exacerbation and further functional decline.

# **Part C: Assessment Findings** {#part-c:-assessment-findings}

The WHODAS 2.0 was administered as part of this Functional Capacity Assessment, with the following results. 

## WHODAS 2.0 {#whodas-2.0}

The World Health Organisation Disability Assessment Schedule 2.0 (WHODAS 2.0) is a standardised measure used to evaluate disability and functional impairment across six domains: cognition, mobility, self-care, getting along with others, life activities and participation. It is a widely recognised and validated tool for assessing the impact of physical and health-related conditions on day-to-day functioning. Scores are converted to a scale from 0 to 100, with higher scores indicating greater levels of disability, and are supported by percentile rankings comparing the individual to international reference populations.

Emma completed the WHODAS 2.0 on 8 January 2026\. His results indicate an overall moderate level of disability, with particularly elevated impairment evident in the domains of mobility and participation, and additional moderate impairment across self-care and life activities. These results are consistent with Emma’s reported functional limitations, medical history and clinical presentation.

Emma’s overall disability score was 49/100, placing him in the 94.7th percentile, meaning his level of disability is greater than approximately 95% of the reference population.

His domain scores are outlined below:

* **Cognition: 30/100 (30%)** This score reflects mild functional impact in cognitive domains such as concentration, memory, learning and problem-solving. While Emma demonstrates generally intact cognition, he reports occasional difficulty sustaining attention, particularly when pain and fatigue are elevated. These difficulties are secondary to physical symptoms rather than a primary cognitive impairment and can affect his ability to maintain focus during prolonged or demanding tasks.  
* **Mobility: 63/100 (63%)** This score indicates moderate functional impairment in mobility-related activities. Emma reports significant difficulty with standing for prolonged periods, walking longer distances and moving around outside the home. These limitations are consistent with his chronic pain, reduced endurance and neurological symptoms, and substantially restrict his ability to navigate environments independently and safely on a sustained basis.  
* **Self-Care: 50/100 (50%)** Emma’s score reflects moderate difficulty with self-care activities. While he is able to complete some personal care tasks independently, there is notable variability within this domain. In particular, dressing was identified as a significant area of difficulty, while other tasks such as staying by himself were reported as less impacted. This variability reflects fluctuating physical capacity influenced by pain, fatigue and neurological symptoms.  
* **Getting Along with Others: 33/100 (33%)** This score indicates mild difficulty in interpersonal functioning. Emma generally maintains appropriate relationships and social interactions however, pain, fatigue and reduced tolerance for prolonged engagement can occasionally affect his ability to participate socially, particularly in unfamiliar or demanding situations.  
* **Life Activities: 50/100 (50%)** This score reflects moderate impairment in managing household responsibilities and daily life activities. Emma reports significant difficulty completing domestic tasks such as cleaning, cooking and maintaining his home environment. These difficulties are directly related to physical limitations, reduced endurance and pain exacerbation with activity, resulting in reliance on his partner to manage these responsibilities.  
* **Participation: 63/100 (63%)** Emma’s participation score indicates moderate restriction in involvement in community activities, social roles and broader life participation. There was notable variability within this domain, with particular difficulty reported in relation to the impact of his health condition on financial resources and emotional wellbeing. These findings highlight the broader social and economic consequences of Emma’s condition and align with his reduced capacity to engage consistently in work and community life.

Emma’s WHODAS 2.0 profile demonstrates a clinically significant level of functional disability, with the greatest impact evident in mobility, participation and life activities, alongside moderate impairment in self-care. These results support the presence of enduring functional limitations that affect Emma’s ability to sustain daily activities, fulfil domestic roles and participate reliably in employment and community contexts. The pattern of results is consistent with a chronic physical condition with permanent functional impact rather than a temporary or resolving impairment.

# **Part D: Summary & Recommendations** {#part-d:-summary-&-recommendations}

## Summary of Functional Impairments {#summary-of-functional-impairments}

Emma is a capable and motivated adult whose functional capacity is significantly impacted by the long-term effects of a permanent cervical spinal injury with neurological involvement. Despite surgical intervention and periods of rehabilitation, Emma continues to experience chronic pain, neurological symptoms, disrupted sleep, reduced endurance and functional limitations that affect his ability to manage daily activities and sustain meaningful roles.

Emma demonstrates relative strengths in cognition and communication, as reflected in clinical observation and supported by his WHODAS 2.0 results, which indicate only mild impairment in these domains. However, his overall functional presentation is dominated by moderate impairment in mobility, self-care, life activities and participation, with these areas having the greatest impact on independence and role fulfilment.

Results from the WHODAS 2.0 indicate an overall moderate level of disability, placing Emma in the 94.7th percentile, meaning his level of disability is greater than approximately 95% of the reference population. The most significant functional limitations were identified in the domains of mobility and participation, reflecting difficulty with prolonged standing and walking, reduced tolerance for sustained activity and restricted involvement in community, vocational and social roles. Moderate impairment in self-care and life activities further highlights Emma’s reduced capacity to manage domestic responsibilities and daily tasks without assistance.

Clinically, Emma’s functional capacity is characterised by variability and limited sustainability. While he may be able to complete individual tasks independently under optimal conditions, his capacity to perform tasks repeatedly, over time, or within the structure of a typical day is significantly compromised. Pain, neurological symptoms and fatigue escalate with physical exertion, sustained postures and repetitive movement, resulting in the need for frequent rest, pacing and task modification.

Sleep disruption further compounds Emma’s functional limitations. Ongoing nocturnal pain and neurological symptoms result in poor sleep quality, contributing to daytime fatigue, increased pain sensitivity and reduced physical and cognitive endurance. This significantly affects his ability to maintain consistency, reliability and stamina across daily activities.

Emma’s domestic and community functioning is also notably impacted. He relies heavily on his partner to manage meal preparation, cleaning, laundry, shopping and physically demanding parenting tasks. Although Emma remains engaged in family life, his physical limitations restrict his ability to participate fully in childcare, household management and recreational activities. Activities that were previously meaningful to him, including sport and physically active leisure, have ceased due to symptom exacerbation.

## Capacity for Employment {#capacity-for-employment}

From an occupational therapy perspective, Emma’s functional profile indicates a significantly reduced capacity for sustained employment. While he has demonstrated strong motivation and made considerable efforts to continue working following his injury, his ability to meet the physical, endurance and reliability demands of employment has progressively declined.

Emma’s condition limits his capacity to tolerate:

* Prolonged sitting or standing  
* Repetitive upper limb use  
* Lifting, carrying and manual handling  
* Sustained concentration when pain and fatigue are elevated  
* Consistent attendance and productivity across a standard workday or workweek

Although Emma may be capable of performing isolated or light tasks intermittently, his functional presentation does not support reliable, safe or sustained engagement in employment. The need for frequent rest breaks, task modification, pacing and recovery time would significantly interfere with work performance, attendance and productivity in any role for which he is reasonably qualified by education, training or experience.

Emma ceased work approximately six months prior to this assessment due to escalating pain, fatigue and reduced functional tolerance and has since sold his business. This reflects the cumulative impact of his condition on his vocational capacity rather than a lack of motivation or willingness to work.

## Clinical Opinion {#clinical-opinion}

Based on clinical assessment, review of medical documentation, functional reporting and standardised assessment results, it is my professional opinion that Emma’s physical condition is permanent in nature and associated with ongoing functional limitations. There is no indication of further significant functional improvement and his presentation is consistent with long-term neurological impairment.

In my opinion as an Occupational Therapist, Emma is unlikely to be able to return to employment for which he is reasonably qualified by education, training or experience, due to the combined impact of chronic pain, neurological symptoms, reduced endurance, disrupted sleep and limited task sustainability. While he may retain capacity for limited, intermittent activity, this does not equate to capacity for ongoing employment.

Accordingly, Emma meets the criteria for permanent incapacity, as his medical condition is likely to prevent him from ever returning to work in roles consistent with his previous vocational background and skill set.
